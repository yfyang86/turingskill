# Michael Stonebraker: Biography

## Early Life and Education
Michael Ralph Stonebraker was born on October 11, 1943, in New Milton, Hampshire, England. His family moved to the United States when he was young, and he grew up in the suburban landscape of New York. He developed an early interest in mathematics and engineering, which led him to pursue higher education in technical fields.

Stonebraker earned his Bachelor's degree in Electrical Engineering from Princeton University in 1965. He then attended the University of Michigan, where he completed his Master's degree in Electrical Engineering in 1966. He continued at Michigan for his doctoral studies, receiving his Ph.D. in Computer Information and Control Engineering in 1971 under the supervision of Archie McKellar and William Opdyke.

## The Berkeley Years: Ingres and Postgres (1971-2000)
Stonebraker joined the University of California, Berkeley as an assistant professor in 1971. It was here that he would create two of the most influential database systems in computing history.

### Ingres (1974-1985)
In 1973, the IBM System R project demonstrated the feasibility of Edgar F. Codd's relational model. Stonebraker, initially skeptical, was convinced by a lecture from Codd and began the Ingres (INteractive Graphics and REtrieval System) project in 1974 with Eugene Wong. Funded by the NSF, ARPA, and the Office of Naval Research, Ingres became one of the first relational database management systems and proved that relational systems could be practical.

### Postgres (1986-1994)
After Ingres, Stonebraker sought to address the limitations of relational systems, particularly for complex data types. Starting in 1986, he led the Postgres (Post Ingres) project, introducing:
- Object-relational capabilities
- Extensible type systems
- Active database rules and triggers
- Support for complex data (geographic, multimedia)

Postgres evolved into PostgreSQL, one of the world's most popular open-source databases.

## Commercial Ventures (1982-2000)
Stonebraker became a serial entrepreneur, founding multiple database companies:

- **Ingres Corporation** (1980): Commercializing Ingres; eventually sold to ASK Computer Systems
- **Illustra** (1992): Object-relational database; acquired by Informix
- **Cohera** (1997): Commercializing PostgreSQL; acquired by PeopleSoft
- **StreamBase Systems** (2003): Stream processing for financial data
- **Vertica** (2005): Column-oriented database for analytics
- **VoltDB** (2009): In-memory database for high-velocity OLTP
- **SciDB** (2009): Array database for scientific computing
- **Tamr** (2013): Data unification using machine learning

## MIT and Continued Innovation (2001-Present)
In 2001, Stonebraker joined MIT as an adjunct professor. At MIT's Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (CSAIL), he continued his research on database architectures, big data, and specialized database systems. He became co-director of the Intel Science and Technology Center for Big Data.

## The 2014 Turing Award
On March 25, 2014, Stonebraker received the ACM Turing Award "for fundamental contributions to the concepts and practices underlying modern database systems." The citation specifically recognized his work on Ingres and Postgres, as well as his leadership in translating academic research into commercial products.

## Philosophy and Character
Stonebraker is known for his willingness to challenge established wisdom. He famously criticized the "one size fits all" approach to database systems, arguing that different applications require different architectures. His advocacy for specialized systems—column stores for analytics, stream processors for real-time data, array databases for scientific computing—has reshaped the database industry.

His career exemplifies the successful intersection of academic research and commercial application, with multiple successful companies validating his technical innovations.
