# Michael O. Rabin: Key Collaborators

## Dana Scott (1957)
The collaboration that produced the Turing Award-winning work:

### The 1957 IBM Summer
- Both were at IBM Research, Yorktown Heights
- Scott was mathematical logician; Rabin was young mathematician
- Joint work on finite automata decision problems
- Led to 1959 paper introducing nondeterministic automata

### Nature of Collaboration
- Relatively brief but extraordinarily impactful
- Mathematical discussions leading to key insights
- Scott's logic background + Rabin's algorithmic thinking
- Remained colleagues and friends over decades

### Joint Impact
The Scott-Rabin paper is one of the most cited in theoretical computer science, providing foundations for automata theory and complexity theory.

## Gary Miller (1975)
Collaboration on primality testing at MIT:

### The Miller-Rabin Test
- Miller had deterministic test assuming Extended Riemann Hypothesis
- Rabin made it probabilistic and unconditional
- Combined number theory expertise
- Result: practical primality testing

### Collaboration Dynamic
- Brief but productive interaction
- Miller's foundation + Rabin's probabilistic insight
- Led to cryptographic standard still in use

## Richard Karp (1987)
String matching algorithm collaboration:

### Rabin-Karp Algorithm
- Hash-based pattern matching
- Average-case efficiency
- Multiple pattern matching capability
- Karp's algorithm expertise + Rabin's hashing insights

### Ongoing Relationship
Both prominent theorists; continued interactions at conferences and in academic community.

## Yonatan Aumann and Yan Zong Ding (2000s)
Hyper-encryption collaboration:

### Provably Secure Encryption
- Combined expertise in cryptography and information theory
- Shared randomness model
- Information-theoretic security results

## Michael Ben-Or (1980s-2000s)
Distributed computing collaboration:

### Byzantine Agreement Work
- Fault-tolerant consensus protocols
- Randomized distributed algorithms
- Asynchronous systems
- Joint Dijkstra Prize (2015)

## Hebrew University Colleagues
During his decades at Hebrew University:
- **Mathematicians**: Interactions with Israeli mathematics community
- **Computer scientists**: Building CS department together
- **Students**: Mentored generations of Israeli theorists

## Harvard Colleagues
As Watson Professor:
- **Theory of Computation group**: Part of Harvard CS theory community
- **Interdisciplinary connections**: With mathematics, applied sciences
- **Students**: Mentored American and international graduate students

## IBM Research Connections
The 1957 summer established ongoing relationships:
- **IBM Watson Research Center**: Returned for various visits
- **Industrial research model**: Appreciated resources and freedom
- **Applied orientation**: Influence on his practical focus

## Student Collaborators
Rabin's students became prominent:
- **Tal Rabin**: His daughter; cryptography researcher
- **Hebrew University students**: Many leading Israeli theorists
- **Harvard students**: Distributed across academic and industrial research

## International Collaborators
Rabin's split position enabled global network:
- **Israeli researchers**: Maintained strong home ties
- **American theorists**: Part of US theoretical CS community
- **European connections**: Through conferences and visiting positions

## Collaboration Patterns
Rabin's collaborative style:
- **Intensive bursts**: Short periods of focused work
- **Mathematical depth**: Theoretical discussions leading to insights
- **Cross-institutional**: Leveraging multiple academic homes
- **Generational**: Working with both senior and junior colleagues
- **International**: Maintaining ties across continents

## The Rabin Network Effect
His collaborations created:
- Primality testing standard (Miller-Rabin)
- String matching standard (Rabin-Karp)
- Cryptographic foundations (Rabin cryptosystem, oblivious transfer)
- Distributed computing theory (Ben-Or-Rabin)
- Multiple generations of students continuing research

Rabin's collaborative legacy shows how individual theoretical insights, when shared and developed with others, can become foundational infrastructure for computer science.
