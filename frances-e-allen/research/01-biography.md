# Frances E. Allen - Biography

## Early Life (1932-1954)

Frances Elizabeth "Fran" Allen was born on August 4, 1932, in Peru, New York, a small farming community near Lake Champlain. She was the eldest of six children in a working-class family. Her father was a farmer, and her mother was an elementary school teacher who instilled a love of learning in her children.

Growing up on a dairy farm, Allen developed a strong work ethic and practical problem-solving skills. She attended the local high school in Peru, where an inspiring mathematics teacher recognized her aptitude and encouraged her to pursue mathematics. This teacher would prove to be a pivotal influence on her life direction.

## Education

Allen attended the New York State College for Teachers (now the State University of New York at Albany) from 1950 to 1954. She earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in mathematics with a minor in physics, while also completing education credentials. Her time at Saint Rose provided a solid foundation in mathematical thinking that would serve her throughout her career.

After graduating, she returned to her hometown to teach mathematics at the same high school she had attended. For two years, she taught everything from elementary algebra to advanced trigonometry. However, she realized that to achieve full certification as a teacher, she needed a master's degree.

Allen enrolled at the University of Michigan at Ann Arbor, where she completed a Master of Science in mathematics in 1957. It was here that she was first exposed to computing through courses taught by Bernard Galler, co-developer of the MAD programming language and future ACM President. She learned to program on an IBM 650, one of the earliest commercial computers.

## IBM Career (1957-2002)

### The Accidental Career (1957)

IBM conducted recruiting interviews on the Michigan campus in 1957. Deeply in debt from student loans, Allen accepted a position at IBM Research with a pragmatic goal: earn enough money to pay off her debts, then return to teaching. She joined IBM on July 15, 1957—exactly two months after the release of FORTRAN, the first high-level programming language.

Her first assignment was teaching FORTRAN to IBM research scientists and engineers. Ironically, she learned the language just days before teaching it, reading the source code of the original FORTRAN compiler developed by John Backus's team. As she later reflected: "It set my interest in compiling, and it also set the way I thought about compilers."

### The Stay That Became 45 Years

What began as a temporary position evolved into a legendary 45-year career. Allen never returned to teaching high school mathematics. Instead, she became one of the most influential computer scientists of the 20th century.

Her early work included the IBM 704's MAD operating system, but her first major project was the Stretch/Harvest supercomputer compiler system (1959-1963). She spent a year at the National Security Agency in 1962 overseeing installation and testing.

Throughout her career, she worked on numerous pioneering projects: the Advanced Computing Systems (ACS) project with John Cocke, the Experimental Compiler Systems (ECS) project, and the Parallel Translator (PTRAN) project that she founded in the 1980s.

## First Female IBM Fellow (1989)

In 1989, Allen was named an IBM Fellow, becoming the first woman to receive this highest technical honor at IBM. This recognition placed her among an elite group of IBM's most distinguished scientists and engineers. She later served as President of the IBM Academy of Technology (1995-1996).

## Personal Life

Frances Allen never married and had no children. She was known for her intense dedication to her work and her privacy. Outside of computing, she was an avid mountain climber and environmentalist, participating in expeditions to Austria, China, Tibet, and the Arctic.

She was briefly married to Jacob "Jack" Schwartz, creator of the SETL programming language, after meeting him during her sabbatical at NYU. The marriage ended, and Allen remained private about her personal life thereafter.

## Death and Legacy (2020)

Frances Allen passed away on August 4, 2020—her 88th birthday—in Schenectady, New York. Her death was announced by IBM, which published a tribute recognizing her contributions to both technology and the advancement of women in computing.

Her nephew Ryan McKee captured her essence: "Fran spent a lifetime working to advance the field of computing and pioneer new breakthroughs. Personally, she was equally focused on inspiring and motivating young people—especially women—to do the same."
