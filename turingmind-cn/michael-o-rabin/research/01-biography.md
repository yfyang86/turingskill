# Michael O. Rabin: Biography

## Early Life and Escape from Nazi Germany
Michael Oser Rabin was born on September 1, 1931, in Breslau, Germany (now Wrocław, Poland). His family recognized the growing danger of Nazism and emigrated to Palestine in 1935 when Michael was four years old. This early experience of displacement and the value of education in a new land shaped his worldview.

## Education in Israel
Rabin grew up in Haifa and showed exceptional mathematical talent from an early age. He attended Hebrew University of Jerusalem, earning his master's degree in mathematics in 1953. His mathematical abilities were recognized by his professors, setting the stage for his future academic career.

## PhD at Princeton (1953-1957)
Rabin went to the United States for doctoral studies at Princeton University, completing his PhD in mathematics in 1957. His dissertation work laid groundwork for his future contributions to logic and automata theory. At Princeton, he was influenced by the rich mathematical environment and the work of Alonzo Church.

## Early Academic Career (1956-1958)
Rabin taught briefly at Princeton (1956-1958) before returning to Israel. This period exposed him to American academic culture and research expectations that would influence his later career.

## Hebrew University (1958-1980)
Returning to Israel, Rabin joined Hebrew University:
- 1958: Appointed professor of mathematics
- Advanced through ranks to senior positions
- Served as Rector (academic head) of the University (1972-1975)
- Founded and chaired the Institute of Mathematics
- Chaired the Computer Science Department

This was a remarkably productive period that saw his most fundamental work in automata theory and primality testing.

## IBM Research Summer (1957)
A pivotal summer at IBM Research in Yorktown Heights, New York, working with Dana Scott, led to their joint paper on finite automata that would win them the Turing Award nearly two decades later.

## MIT and Harvard (1975-present)
Rabin expanded to American institutions:
- 1975: Visiting professor at MIT; developed Miller-Rabin primality test with Gary Miller
- 1981: Gordon McKay Professor of Computer Science, Harvard
- 1983: Thomas J. Watson, Sr. Professor of Computer Science, Harvard
- 2013: Thomas J. Watson, Sr. Research Professor

This split position (Hebrew University and Harvard) allowed him to maintain ties to Israel while participating in American academia.

## Key Research Periods

### Automata Theory (1957-1960s)
The work with Scott on nondeterministic finite automata provided theoretical foundations for computer science.

### Randomized Algorithms (1970s-1980s)
At MIT in 1975, Rabin's work on probabilistic primality testing opened new algorithmic paradigms.

### Cryptography (1980s-1990s)
Developed oblivious transfer (1981), hyper-encryption (with Aumann and Ding), and other cryptographic protocols.

### Distributed Computing (1980s-2000s)
Work with Michael Ben-Or on fault-tolerant distributed algorithms.

## Honors and Awards
- 1976: ACM Turing Award (with Dana Scott)
- 1980: Harvey Prize
- 1995: Israel Prize in Computer Science
- 2003: ACM Paris Kanellakis Award (primality testing)
- 2010: Dan David Prize
- 2015: Dijkstra Prize (distributed computing)
- Member: US National Academy of Sciences, American Academy of Arts and Sciences, Israeli Academy of Sciences, Royal Society (UK)

## Personal Life
Rabin's daughter, Tal Rabin, is also a distinguished computer scientist, continuing the family tradition. Rabin has maintained connections to both Israel and the United States throughout his career, embodying the international nature of modern science.

## Character and Research Style
Rabin is known for:
- Elegant mathematical solutions to practical problems
- Willingness to tackle fundamental questions across multiple areas
- Movement between pure theory and practical applications
- Maintaining research productivity across decades
- Mentoring students in both Israel and the US

His career demonstrates how theoretical computer science can address both fundamental mathematical questions and practical cryptographic needs.
