# Yoshua Bengio: Conversations and Interviews

## The 2018 Turing Award Interview

### On Receiving the Award
"This award recognizes not just the three of us, but the entire community that kept neural networks alive during the long winter. When Geoff, Yann, and I started working on this, many people thought neural networks were a dead end. We persisted because we believed in the fundamental principles."

### On the Neural Network Winter
"The 1990s and early 2000s were difficult. Funding was scarce, conferences were dominated by other approaches, and it was hard to get papers published. But the few of us who continued believed that learning representations from data was the right path. We were preparing for when the data and compute would be available."

### On the Breakthrough
"The breakthrough came gradually—better algorithms, more data, faster computers. The 2012 ImageNet result was the moment the wider world noticed, but the foundations had been laid over decades. It's satisfying to see these ideas finally transforming technology."

## Interview on Deep Learning Principles

### On Why Deep Learning Works
"Deep learning works because it learns representations—hierarchical abstractions that capture the structure of data. Each layer learns increasingly abstract features. This is similar to how the brain processes information, though the details differ."

### On Unsupervised Learning
"The future is in unsupervised and self-supervised learning. We learn from massive amounts of unlabeled data, just as children do. Labeled data is scarce, but unlabeled data is abundant. Learning to represent the structure of the world without explicit labels is key."

### On the Limitations
"Current deep learning has important limitations. We need causal understanding, not just correlation. We need systematic generalization, not just pattern matching. We need to integrate System 2 capabilities—deliberate reasoning. There's much work to do."

## Interview on AI Safety

### On AI Risk
"I started focusing more on safety because the stakes are so high. As AI systems become more powerful, ensuring they remain aligned with human values becomes critical. This is a technical problem that requires research, not just regulation."

### On Long-term Risks
"In the long term, we need to solve the alignment problem—ensuring that AI systems pursue goals that are beneficial to humanity. This is challenging because specifying human values precisely is difficult, and powerful optimization can find unexpected solutions."

### On Current Work
"My recent work focuses on causal representation learning and System 2 capabilities. We need AI systems that understand causation, that can reason deliberately, that don't just pattern-match but actually understand."

## Interview on Canadian AI

### On Montreal as AI Hub
"Montreal becoming an AI hub wasn't accidental. We invested in fundamental research for decades, trained students, built institutions like MILA. When deep learning took off, we were ready. The Canadian AI strategy helped accelerate this."

### On CIFAR
"CIFAR's support was crucial during the difficult years. They funded long-term, high-risk research when others wouldn't. This is exactly what public research funding should do—support fundamental research that industry might not fund."

### On Element AI
"Element AI was an experiment in translating research to economic value while maintaining ethical standards. It showed that we can build AI companies in Canada. The sale to ServiceNow was the right outcome for the company and its employees."

## Interview on Education and Mentorship

### On Teaching
"Teaching is one of the most rewarding parts of my job. Seeing students understand deep concepts, watching them develop into independent researchers—it's wonderful. The students at MILA are exceptional."

### On the Deep Learning Book
"Writing the deep learning book with Ian and Aaron was a major undertaking. We wanted to make the knowledge accessible to everyone. Making it freely available online was important—we didn't want cost to be a barrier to learning."

### On Advice to Students
"Follow your curiosity. Work on problems that genuinely interest you, not just what's fashionable. Learn the fundamentals deeply. And collaborate—science is a social activity, and you'll go further with good collaborators."

## Interview on AI Ethics

### On Responsibility
"As AI researchers, we have a responsibility to think about the consequences of our work. We're not just developing technology in a vacuum—we're shaping tools that will affect billions of people."

### On Bias and Fairness
"AI systems can perpetuate or amplify biases in training data. We need to be aware of this, measure it, and develop techniques to mitigate it. Fairness in AI is both a technical and a social challenge."

### On the Montreal Declaration
"The Montreal Declaration was an attempt to articulate principles for responsible AI development. It's not legally binding, but it sets aspirational goals. We need both ethical frameworks and technical solutions."
