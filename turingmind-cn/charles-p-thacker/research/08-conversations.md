# Charles P. Thacker: Conversations and Interviews

## Turing Award Interview (2010)
**Context**: Interview following 2009 Turing Award
**Published**: Communications of the ACM, 2010

**Key Exchanges**:

**On the Alto's creation**:
> "Alan Kay had this vision of the Dynabook—a personal computer for children. Butler Lampson and I looked at what was possible with current technology and designed the Alto to be a step toward that vision. We knew it was important, but we didn't realize how transformative it would be."

**On collaboration at PARC**:
> "PARC was an extraordinary place. We had brilliant people—Kay, Lampson, Metcalfe, Geschke, Warnock—and the management gave us freedom to pursue ambitious ideas. The Alto wasn't my work alone; it was the result of a team all pushing in the same direction."

**On Ethernet**:
> "Bob Metcalfe had the protocol idea, and I worked on the hardware implementation. We wanted something simple that would work reliably. The beauty of Ethernet was its simplicity—coaxial cable, simple transceivers, elegant protocol."

## Computer History Museum Oral History (2007)
**Context**: Extended oral history interview
**Topics**: Career, PARC years, hardware design philosophy

**On hardware design**:
> "I always believed in building things that actually work. You can simulate, you can analyze, but until you have hardware running real software with real users, you don't really know if you've got it right."

**On the personal computer revolution**:
> "We built the Alto as a research tool, but we knew we were onto something. When Steve Jobs visited PARC and saw what we had, that accelerated everything. Apple brought personal computing to the masses."

## Microsoft Research Interview (2000s)
**Context**: Internal Microsoft Research interview

**On joining Microsoft**:
> "After DEC, I wanted to keep doing interesting hardware work. Microsoft Research gave me the freedom to explore new ideas with great colleagues. It's not PARC in the 1970s, but it's a good environment for research."

**On continued relevance**:
> "Hardware keeps changing—new processes, new architectures—but the fundamentals remain. You need clean design, careful implementation, and attention to the user's needs."

## ACM Queue Interview (2009)
**Context**: Interview about career and influences

**On influences**:
> "Wesley Clark and the LINC had a big influence on my thinking about personal computing. The PDP-1 at MIT showed what interactive computing could be. I was building on foundations others had laid."

**On the future of computing**:
> "The challenges now are different—power consumption, parallelism, mobile form factors. But the principles are the same: understand the problem, design elegantly, implement carefully."

## Panel Discussions

### "PARC Revisited" Panel (2000s)
**Context**: With Kay, Lampson, and other PARC alumni

**On PARC's culture**:
> "We were given resources and freedom that would be hard to find today. Xerox didn't always know what to do with our inventions, but they let us pursue them. That freedom was precious."

**On regrets**:
> "I wish Xerox had commercialized the Alto more aggressively. We had a ten-year lead on the industry, and much of that was lost. But the ideas got out, and that's what matters."

## Interview Style
Thacker in interviews:
- Humble about personal contributions
- Generous in crediting collaborators
- Technically specific when discussing hardware
- Reflective about historical context
- Forward-looking despite age

## Notable Quotes

On engineering:
> "The best engineering is invisible. When everything works as it should, users don't think about the hardware—they just get their work done."

On innovation:
> "Innovation happens when you have a clear vision and the skills to implement it. At PARC, we had both."

On legacy:
> "I hope I'm remembered as someone who helped make computing accessible to everyone. The personal computer revolution changed the world, and I was fortunate to be part of it."
