# John L. Hennessy: Conversations and Interviews

## The 2017 Turing Award Interview

### On Receiving the Award with David Patterson
"Dave and I have been working in this field for four decades, and it's gratifying to see the principles we developed become so widely adopted. The RISC approach is now the standard, whether people call it that or not. ARM processors, modern x86 implementations—they all use the principles we articulated in the 1980s."

### On the RISC Revolution
"When we started, the conventional wisdom was that more complex instruction sets were better. We took a different approach—simplify the hardware, let the compiler do the work. It was controversial at the time, but the quantitative evidence was clear. Simpler, pipelined processors could achieve higher performance."

### On the Textbook
"The textbook with Dave came from our shared belief that computer architecture should be taught as a quantitative discipline. We wanted students to analyze trade-offs, to measure and model, not just memorize design descriptions. It seems obvious now, but at the time, architecture was taught much more qualitatively."

## Stanford Presidential Interview (2016)

### On Leaving the Presidency
"Sixteen years is a long time, and it felt like the right moment for new leadership. Stanford is in an excellent position—academically strong, financially sound, and well-positioned for the future. I'm proud of what we accomplished, but institutions outlast individuals. It's important to know when to step aside."

### On Stanford and Silicon Valley
"Stanford and Silicon Valley grew up together. The relationship is symbiotic—we educate the talent, generate ideas, and maintain our commitment to open research. But we must also maintain our independence. We're a university first, not an R&D lab for industry. That balance requires constant attention."

### On Fundraising
"The Stanford Challenge raised over $6 billion, but it's never just about the money. It's about engaging our community—alumni, friends, foundations—in our mission. People give because they believe in what Stanford does: educate students, create knowledge, and contribute to society."

## Interview on Academic Entrepreneurship

### On Founding MIPS
"Leaving academia to start MIPS was a difficult decision. I believed in the technology, but I also had obligations to Stanford and my students. We structured it carefully—I took leave, maintained separation between company and university research, and returned to full-time academic work when the time was right."

### On the University Role
"Universities have a responsibility to translate research into benefit for society. Sometimes that happens through publications and education. Sometimes it happens through technology transfer and entrepreneurship. Both are legitimate, and universities should support both."

### On Student Startups
"Stanford has been extraordinarily successful in generating entrepreneurial ventures. Google, founded by our graduate students, is just the most prominent example. We try to create an environment where students can pursue those paths while maintaining academic integrity."

## Interview on the Future of Computing

### On Moore's Law
"Moore's Law has been the defining trend of our field, but it's slowing. We're entering a new era where architectural innovation—like the RISC approach we pioneered—becomes more important than process scaling. Specialized accelerators, domain-specific architectures, new memory technologies—these will drive future progress."

### On Education
"The fundamentals remain important—algorithms, computer architecture, systems. But students also need breadth. The big challenges—in health, energy, environment—require interdisciplinary approaches. Computer scientists need to work with biologists, chemists, economists, and others."

### On Responsibility
"Technology is powerful, and with power comes responsibility. We need to think about the implications of what we build—privacy, security, fairness, employment. These aren't just technical problems; they're social problems that require technical insight."

## Conversation at Computer History Museum

### On the MIPS Experience
"Building MIPS was a learning experience. Academic research prepares you for the technical challenges, but running a company requires entirely different skills—raising money, building teams, dealing with customers, competing in the market. I learned a tremendous amount that served me well later."

### On Collaboration with Patterson
"Dave and I have been colleagues, competitors, and collaborators for decades. The competition between Stanford and Berkeley RISC projects drove both to be better. The collaboration on the textbook brought our perspectives together. It's been a productive relationship that I'm grateful for."

### On Legacy
"If there's a legacy, I hope it's the methodology—quantitative, rigorous, evidence-based. And the textbook that continues to influence new generations. The specific architectures matter less than the approach to thinking about architecture."
