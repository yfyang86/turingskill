# Alan J. Perlis - Key Decisions

## Career Decisions

### Moving to Carnegie Mellon (1956)
- Left Tufts for Carnegie Institute of Technology
- Opportunity to build computation center
- Foundation for future CS department
- Decision shaped U.S. computer science landscape

### Creating CS Department (1965)
- Founded first computer science department in U.S.
- Risk of establishing new academic discipline
- Convinced university administration of importance
- Model copied by universities worldwide

### Move to Yale (1971)
- Left established department for new challenge
- Rebuilt career at new institution
- Established Yale CS department
- Expanded influence to East Coast

## Technical Decisions

### ALGOL Commitment (1958)
- Led U.S. participation in international effort
- Chose ALGOL over proprietary alternatives
- Advocated for mathematical elegance in languages
- Influenced entire programming language trajectory

### IT Compiler Design (1956)
- Developed Internal Translator at Carnegie Tech
- Early algebraic compiler architecture
- Decision to focus on correctness over optimization
- Foundation for his compiler research

### Curriculum 68 (1968)
- Led ACM curriculum committee
- Defined core CS knowledge areas
- Balanced theory and practice
- Influenced CS education for decades

## Philosophical Decisions

### Epigrams Publication (1982)
- Published 130 programming aphorisms
- Risk of appearing unserious
- Became most widely read work
- Shaped programming culture

### Turing Award Acceptance (1966)
- First recipient set tone for future
- Lecture emphasized synthesis of systems
- Positioned CS as legitimate discipline
- Validated field's importance

## Teaching Decisions

### Pedagogical Approach
- Emphasized programming as intellectual discipline
- Rejected vocational training model
- Required mathematical foundations
- Demanded excellence from students

### Department Culture
- Created research-focused environment
- Balanced theory with systems building
- Emphasized collaboration over competition
- Mentored junior faculty

## Collaboration Decisions

### ALGOL Committee Leadership
- Engaged with international colleagues
- Navigated competing national interests
- Balanced innovation with consensus
- Maintained relationships despite disagreements

### Industry Partnerships
- Collaborated with IBM, Burroughs
- Maintained academic independence
- Valued practical feedback
- Avoided exclusive arrangements

## Legacy Decisions

### Academic vs. Industry Path
- Chose academic career despite industry opportunities
- Focused on education and research
- Built institutions rather than companies
- Influence through students and departments

### Writing and Communication
- Invested time in clear exposition
- Published for broad audience
- Used humor to reach readers
- Prioritized education over technical complexity
