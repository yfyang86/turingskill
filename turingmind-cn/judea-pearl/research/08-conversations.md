# Judea Pearl: Conversations and Interviews

## Turing Award Interview (2012)

**On causal inference**:
> "For decades, statisticians avoided causation, saying 'correlation is not causation.' But they didn't provide tools for causal reasoning. I wanted to give scientists the mathematical language to talk about causation rigorously."

**On Bayesian networks**:
> "Probabilistic reasoning was computationally intractable. Bayesian networks provided structure that made inference feasible. This enabled practical AI systems."

## "The Book of Why" Interviews (2018)

**On making causation accessible**:
> "Everyone understands causation intuitively. My goal was to show that there's rigorous mathematics behind this intuition, and that it can solve real problems."

**On the ladder of causation**:
> "We reason at three levels: seeing (association), doing (intervention), and imagining (counterfactuals). Each requires different mathematical machinery."

## CACM Interview (2011)

**On statistics and causation**:
> "Statistics textbooks taught generations of scientists to avoid causal language. This was a mistake—we need causal thinking for science and policy."

**On controversy**:
> "Challenging orthodoxy is uncomfortable, but necessary. The potential outcomes camp and I have differences, but we're all trying to understand causation better."

## Daniel Pearl Foundation Context

**On his son**:
> "Daniel believed in dialogue between cultures. The foundation honors his memory by promoting the understanding he championed."

**On responding to tragedy**:
> "You can let tragedy destroy you, or you can build something positive from it. Daniel would have wanted us to choose the latter."

## Interview Style
Pearl in interviews:
- Passionate about causation
- Clear explanations of complex ideas
- Respectful of critics
- Personal warmth
- Philosophical depth

## Notable Quotes

On causation:
> "Causation is not a mystery. It's a mathematical relationship that can be precisely defined and computed."

On AI:
> "Current AI lacks causal understanding. True intelligence requires reasoning about causes and effects."

On science:
> "Science is about understanding causes. Without causal reasoning, we're just describing patterns."
