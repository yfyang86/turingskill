# David A. Patterson: Conversations and Interviews

## The 2017 Turing Award Interview

### On Receiving the Award with John Hennessy
"When John and I started working on RISC in the early 1980s, we were challenging the established wisdom. Everyone believed that more complex instruction sets were better. We showed that simpler was faster, but only if you did the engineering right. Forty years later, every processor uses these ideas. It's gratifying to see."

### On the RISC vs. CISC Debate
"The debate was heated at times, but it was productive. Intel proved that you could make CISC work through heroic implementation. We showed that RISC was fundamentally cleaner. In the end, everyone won—modern processors use RISC principles internally, whether they're called RISC or not."

### On the Textbook
"The textbook with John came from frustration with how architecture was taught. We wanted students to analyze and measure, not just memorize. It's been through six editions now, and we're still learning how to teach this material better."

## Interview on RAID Development

### On the RAID Idea
"The RAID idea came from frustration with expensive disks. We realized you could use many cheap disks, add some redundancy, and get better performance and reliability than expensive storage. The key insight was that redundancy could be provided in software, not just hardware."

### On RAID Impact
"I never expected RAID to become so ubiquitous. Every data center, every cloud service, every enterprise storage system uses RAID or something derived from it. It's rewarding when a research idea becomes infrastructure that everyone uses."

### On Collaboration with Katz and Gibson
"Randy Katz and I had been thinking about storage, and Garth Gibson came to us with ideas for his thesis. The three of us worked together to develop the RAID concept and the taxonomy of levels. It was a great collaboration."

## Conversation on Teaching and Education

### On Teaching Philosophy
"I love teaching. There's nothing better than seeing students get excited about ideas. Computer architecture is especially fun because you can see the results—you build something, measure it, and understand why it performs the way it does."

### On "How to Have a Bad Career"
"That talk started as real advice—I kept seeing students make the same mistakes. Then I realized it would be more memorable if I framed it negatively, as things to avoid. It's become surprisingly popular. I guess everyone likes to learn from others' mistakes."

### On Mentoring Students
"The best part of being a professor is working with bright students. I've been incredibly lucky—the students who came through my group have gone on to do amazing things. It's their success, not mine, but I'm proud to have been part of their journey."

## Interview on Google Experience

### On Moving to Google
"After 40 years at Berkeley, Google was a new adventure. The scale is mind-boggling—billions of users, millions of machines. Everything you thought you knew about systems gets challenged when you operate at that scale."

### On Warehouse-Scale Computing
"At Google, we started thinking of the data center as a computer. It's not about individual machines; it's about the aggregate. That perspective changes everything—how you design software, how you handle failures, how you think about performance."

### On Industry vs. Academia
"Industry and academia are different but complementary. Academia lets you explore long-term ideas. Industry makes you confront reality at scale. I've been lucky to experience both."

## Interview on Computing Future

### On Moore's Law
"Moore's Law has been the engine of our field, but it's slowing. That means architecture becomes more important. We can't just rely on process technology to give us free performance. We have to design smarter."

### On Machine Learning Hardware
"The success of deep learning has created demand for specialized hardware. TPUs, GPUs, and custom accelerators are becoming essential. It's a return to the kind of hardware-software co-design we did with RISC."

### On Cloud Computing
"Cloud computing is the biggest change in how we use computers since the PC. It enables things that were impossible before—massive scale, global distribution, rapid deployment. And it's still evolving."
