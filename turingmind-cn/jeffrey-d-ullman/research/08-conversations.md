# Jeffrey D. Ullman: Interviews and Dialogues

## On Meeting Alfred Aho

> "We met while waiting in the registration line on our first day of graduate school at Princeton in 1963. Neither of us could have imagined that chance encounter would lead to a 50-year collaboration."

## On Bell Labs

> "Bell Labs in the 1960s was an extraordinary place. You had the freedom to pursue long-term research, and management trusted that good science would eventually find applications."

## On Leaving Princeton for Stanford

> "I was frustrated by Princeton's treatment of computer science. They wouldn't allow us to have our own department. Computing was seen merely as a service to 'real' disciplines. Stanford offered a proper home for CS research."

## On the Dragon Book

> "In many ways, those textbooks, particularly the Dragon Book, brought a lot of new people to computer science. At some point, kids were proud to be seen walking around campus with that cover under their arm."

## On Textbook Writing

> "Never take more than a year to write a book. If you do, the reason you are writing it will have passed. The field moves too quickly to spend five years on a textbook."

## On Sergey Brin

> "Sergey was one of my PhD students, though he left before completing his degree. Obviously, he made the right decision for himself. It's satisfying to see students succeed, even when they take unexpected paths."

## On Database Theory

> "When I started working on database theory in the 1970s, databases were considered an engineering problem, not a subject for theoretical study. We showed that formal foundations could lead to better systems."

## On Long-term Collaboration

> "Jeff [Aho] and I have different strengths—I tend toward the theoretical, he toward the practical. This complementarity has worked well for five decades. We meet regularly, discuss ideas, and the books gradually come together."

## On Gradiance

> "I started Gradiance to provide better tools for CS education—automatic grading, immediate feedback. Textbooks alone aren't enough; students need practice and assessment."

## On Receiving the Turing Award

> "It's an honor to share this with my longtime collaborator Alfred Aho. The citation mentions our books, which reflects our belief that making knowledge accessible is as important as creating it."

## On Computer Science Education

> "CS evolves so quickly that textbooks must balance enduring principles with current practice. That's why we've updated the Dragon Book three times—the foundations remain, but the applications change."

## On Industry vs. Academia

> "Academia gives you freedom to pursue long-term questions. Industry gives you real problems and resources. I've been fortunate to experience both—Bell Labs offered a rare combination of both worlds."

## On the Future of Databases

> "The challenges keep evolving. We moved from relational theory to distributed systems to web data to big data. The fundamental principles—data independence, query optimization, integrity—remain relevant, but the scale and heterogeneity create new problems."

## Advice to Students

> "Learn the fundamentals deeply. Specific technologies change, but the underlying principles—algorithms, data structures, theory—endure. And learn to write clearly. Your work has no impact if others can't understand it."

## On MapReduce and Big Data

> "When MapReduce emerged, we recognized it as a new paradigm for large-scale computation. Our work on join processing in MapReduce environments showed how database theory applies to these new architectures."
