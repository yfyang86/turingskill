# Bob Metcalfe: Interviews and Dialogues

## On Inventing Ethernet

> "I was trying to connect personal computers together. At Xerox PARC, we had these Alto computers, and they needed to talk to each other and to printers. The ALOHA network from Hawaii gave me the idea for how to share a medium."

## On the Name "Ethernet"

> "I named it Ethernet to suggest that the medium was the ether—like the luminiferous ether that 19th-century physicists thought carried light waves. It was a bit of a physics joke."

## On Open Standards

> "We decided to make Ethernet an open standard rather than a Xerox proprietary technology. That was crucial. If we had kept it closed, Token Ring or something else would have won. Openness enabled Ethernet to become ubiquitous."

## On 3Com

> "I left Xerox to start 3Com because I realized that to really make Ethernet happen, someone needed to build the products—Ethernet cards for PCs, hubs, software. That's what we did at 3Com."

## On Metcalfe's Law

> "The value of a network grows as the square of the number of users. That's why the Internet became so valuable—everyone was on it. It's also why Facebook and other platforms became so dominant."

## On the Internet Collapse Prediction

> "I predicted the Internet would collapse in 1996. I was wrong. At a conference, I literally ate my words—blended up a copy of my column and ate it. When you're wrong, admit it. That's how you maintain credibility."

## On Ethernet's Longevity

> "Ethernet has lasted 50 years because it evolved. We went from 3 Mbps to 400 Gbps, from coaxial cable to fiber, but the basic idea—packet switching with CSMA/CD—remained. Evolution beats revolution."

## On Innovation

> "The best way to predict the future is to invent it. Don't just write papers about what might happen—build it, deploy it, make it real."

## On Standards

> "Standards are powerful. A good standard creates an ecosystem. A bad standard fragments a market. We were lucky with Ethernet—it became a good standard because it was open and simple."

## On Competition

> "Token Ring was technically superior in some ways, but Ethernet was open, cheaper, and good enough. That's often the winning combination in technology—good enough and open beats perfect and proprietary."

## On Being a Pundit

> "At InfoWorld, I learned that having a platform means having responsibility. People listen to what you say. You have to be interesting enough to get attention, but responsible enough not to misuse it."

## On Venture Capital

> "After 3Com, I became a venture capitalist. I wanted to help the next generation of entrepreneurs build companies. The best part is working with smart people who are passionate about their ideas."

## On the Turing Award

> "It's an incredible honor to receive the Turing Award. I'm grateful to the ACM and to everyone who contributed to Ethernet's success—David Boggs, my colleagues at Xerox, the DIX partners, the IEEE standards committee."

## Advice to Entrepreneurs

> "Find a problem worth solving. Build a solution that works. Convince others to join you. And don't be afraid to make predictions—just be willing to admit when you're wrong."

## On the Future

> "The networking challenges today are different—5G, IoT, edge computing—but the principles remain. We need open standards, interoperability, and simplicity. That's what made Ethernet successful."
