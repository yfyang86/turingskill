# Dana S. Scott: Biography

## Early Life and Education
Dana Stewart Scott was born on October 11, 1932, in Berkeley, California - the same city where he would eventually retire. He grew up in the San Francisco Bay Area during the Depression and World War II. Scott showed early mathematical aptitude and participated in the Westinghouse Science Talent Search in 1950, foreshadowing his scientific career.

## University of California, Berkeley (1950-1954)
Scott attended UC Berkeley, earning his bachelor's degree in mathematics in 1954. He began studying logic with Alfred Tarski, though they had a temporary falling out. Tarski later told Scott, "I hope I can call you my student," acknowledging their reconciliation and Scott's achievements.

## Princeton and Alonzo Church (1954-1958)
Scott pursued graduate studies at Princeton University under the legendary logician Alonzo Church (known for the lambda calculus and Church-Turing thesis). He completed his PhD in 1958 with a dissertation on foundational issues in logic.

## Early Academic Career (1958-1972)
Scott held positions at several prestigious institutions:
- **1958-1960**: University of Chicago (Instructor)
- **1960-1963**: UC Berkeley (Assistant to Associate Professor)
- **1963-1969**: Stanford University (Associate to Full Professor)
- **1968-1969**: Visiting Professor, University of Amsterdam
- **1969-1972**: Princeton University (Professor of Philosophy and Mathematics)

This period established him as a leading mathematical logician.

## Oxford Years (1972-1981)
Scott became the first Professor of Mathematical Logic at Oxford University, holding a professorial fellowship at Merton College. This period saw crucial collaboration with Christopher Strachey on programming language semantics.

## Domain Theory and Programming Languages (1969-1972)
While at Stanford and Princeton, Scott developed the breakthrough work on domain theory:
- Mathematical models for the lambda calculus
- Foundations for denotational semantics
- Solutions to the problem of self-application in functions
- Type theory advances

## Carnegie Mellon (1981-2003)
Scott joined CMU in 1981:
- University Professor of Computer Science, Mathematical Logic, and Philosophy
- Hillman Professor of Computer Science (1989-2003)
- Emeritus since 2003

This long tenure allowed him to influence generations of researchers.

## Key Collaborations

### Michael Rabin (1957)
The IBM summer collaboration produced the automata work that won the Turing Award.

### Christopher Strachey (1970s)
The Oxford collaboration created denotational semantics:
- Mathematical meaning for programs
- Compositional semantics
- Foundation for programming language theory

### Logicians and Philosophers
Ongoing interactions with:
- Alonzo Church (his advisor)
- Kurt Gödel (correspondence)
- Various modal and tense logicians

## Honors and Awards
- **1972**: Leroy P. Steele Prize (AMS)
- **1976**: ACM Turing Award (with Michael Rabin)
- **1990**: Harold Pender Award (University of Pennsylvania)
- **1997**: Rolf Schock Prize in Logic and Philosophy (Royal Swedish Academy)
- **2001**: Bolzano Medal (Czech Academy)
- **2007**: EATCS Award
- **2009**: Russian Academy Gold Medal
- Member: National Academy of Sciences, British Academy, American Academy of Arts and Sciences, Academia Europaea

## Later Years and Retirement
After retiring from CMU in 2003, Scott returned to Berkeley. He has remained active in:
- Modal logic research
- Programming language theory
- Philosophy of mathematics
- Educational activities

## Personal Characteristics
Scott is known for:
- Gentle demeanor and kindness
- Intellectual generosity
- Breadth across logic, mathematics, and computer science
- Continued engagement with fundamental questions
- Patient mentorship of students

His career illustrates how deep mathematical logic can transform computer science practice.
