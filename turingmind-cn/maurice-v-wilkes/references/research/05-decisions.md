# Maurice V. Wilkes - Key Decisions

## Career Decisions

### Leading Cambridge Mathematical Laboratory (1945)
- Returned from WWII service to take leadership
- Laboratory was small, had single differential analyzer
- Decision to build stored-program computer
- Established Cambridge as computing center

### Attending Moore School Lectures (1946)
- Traveled to U.S. for summer lectures
- Learned about ENIAC and EDVAC
- Met American computing pioneers
- Brought back von Neumann architecture concepts

### Building EDSAC (1946-1949)
- Committed to stored-program design
- Worked with limited post-war resources
- Focused on practicality over speed
- Decision to complete quickly (3 years)

### Inventing Microprogramming (1951)
- Recognized control unit complexity problem
- Developed microprogramming solution
- Initially kept as Cambridge technique
- Published in 1955 after validation

## Technical Decisions

### EDSAC Design Choices
- Chose mercury delay line memory
- 1024-word memory (practical choice)
- Subroutine library approach
- Focus on reliability over performance

### EDSAC 2 Architecture
- Committed to microprogramming
- Bit-slice design approach
- Index registers for arrays
- Influenced by practical experience

### Publishing Strategy
- Wrote first programming textbook (1951)
- Published detailed technical papers
- Shared designs openly
- Built international reputation

## Academic Decisions

### Department Development
- Grew Mathematical Laboratory to Computer Laboratory
- Hired Roger Needham as successor
- Balanced research and teaching
- Supported PhD program development

### Curriculum Development
- Created practical computing courses
- Emphasized hands-on experience
- Built EDSAC for student use
- Model for CS education

### Industry Relations
- Consulted while maintaining independence
- Supported Ferranti commercial computers
- Balanced academic and industrial work
- Avoided exclusive arrangements

## Collaboration Decisions

### International Engagement
- Attended international conferences
- Hosted visitors from worldwide
- Collaborated with American researchers
- Bridge between British and American computing

### Student Mentorship
- Hired David Wheeler and others
- Supported student autonomy
- Shared credit generously
- Long-term mentorship relationships

### Collegial Relations
- Friendly rivalry with Manchester
- Open sharing with American colleagues
- Supported British computing community
- ACM and IEEE participation

## Legacy Decisions

### Writing Memoirs (1985)
- Documented early computing history
- Preserved institutional memory
- Acknowledged colleagues' contributions
- Historical responsibility

### Retirement Timing (1986)
- Stepped down as Lab head at 67
- Remained active in retirement
- Supported successor transition
- Emeritus professorship

- Supported Computer Laboratory archives
