# Shafi Goldwasser: Biography

## Early Life and Education
Shafrira "Shafi" Goldwasser was born in 1958 in New York City, though she spent much of her childhood in Israel. She developed an early interest in mathematics and theoretical computer science. Goldwasser attended Carnegie Mellon University, earning her Bachelor's degree in Mathematics in 1979. She then pursued graduate studies at the University of California, Berkeley, where she worked under the supervision of Manuel Blum (who would later also win the Turing Award).

At Berkeley, Goldwasser made foundational contributions to cryptography that would define her career. She received her Master's degree in 1981 and her Ph.D. in Computer Science in 1984.

## MIT and Weizmann Institute (1983-present)
In 1983, while completing her Ph.D., Goldwasser joined the faculty at MIT. She has remained at MIT throughout her career, eventually becoming the RSA Professor of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science. She also holds a professorship at the Weizmann Institute of Science in Israel, maintaining strong connections to her homeland.

## Foundational Contributions
Goldwasser made several revolutionary contributions to cryptography and theoretical computer science:

### Zero-Knowledge Proofs (1985)
With Silvio Micali and Charles Rackoff, Goldwasser invented zero-knowledge proofs—methods to prove knowledge without revealing the knowledge itself. This seemingly paradoxical concept became fundamental to modern cryptography.

### Probabilistic Encryption (1982-1984)
With Micali, Goldwasser developed the theory of probabilistic encryption, showing how to achieve semantic security—the gold standard for encryption security.

### Interactive Proof Systems
Goldwasser, Micali, and Rackoff defined interactive proof systems and the IP complexity class, leading to major advances in complexity theory including the PCP theorem.

### Other Contributions
- Multi-party secure computation
- Pseudo-random functions
- Average-case complexity
- Property testing

## 2012 Turing Award
Goldwasser shared the 2012 ACM A.M. Turing Award with Silvio Micali "for transformative work that laid the complexity-theoretic foundations for the science of cryptography." They were recognized for:
- Zero-knowledge proofs
- Probabilistic encryption
- Interactive proof systems
- Making cryptography a rigorous science

## Women in Computing
Goldwasser became the third woman to win the Turing Award (after Frances Allen and Barbara Liskov). She has been an advocate for women in computer science throughout her career.

## Honors and Recognition
- 2012: ACM Turing Award (with Silvio Micali)
- 2012: IEEE Emanuel R. Piore Award (with Micali)
- Member of National Academy of Sciences
- Member of National Academy of Engineering
- Fellow of ACM, IEEE, American Academy of Arts and Sciences
- Multiple honorary doctorates

## Research Style
Goldwasser is known for:
- **Mathematical rigor**: Precise definitions and proofs
- **Conceptual boldness**: Tackling seemingly impossible problems
- **Collaborative spirit**: Deep partnerships with Micali and others
- **Interdisciplinary reach**: Connecting cryptography to complexity theory
- **Mentorship**: Training outstanding students

## Legacy
Shafi Goldwasser transformed cryptography from an art to a science. Her work on zero-knowledge proofs and probabilistic encryption underpins modern secure communication, digital signatures, and blockchain technologies. She represents the power of rigorous theoretical research to enable practical security.
