# Herbert A. Simon: Key Decisions and Their Impact

## Career-Defining Decisions

### 1. Choosing Political Science (1933)
**Context**: Undergraduate at University of Chicago
**Decision**: Major in political science rather than economics or hard science
**Impact**: Led to organization theory focus; later brought behavioral insights to economics
**Alternative**: Economics major might have produced different but perhaps narrower contributions

### 2. Writing "Administrative Behavior" (1940s)
**Context**: PhD dissertation at Chicago
**Decision**: Challenge classical management theory with bounded rationality
**Impact**: Revolutionized organization theory; 50+ years of influence
**Risk**: Attacking orthodoxy could have damaged academic career

### 3. Moving to Carnegie Tech (1949)
**Context**: Offer from new Graduate School of Industrial Administration
**Decision**: Leave Illinois Tech for Pittsburgh
**Impact**: Environment enabling interdisciplinary work; meeting Newell
**Trade-off**: GSIA was new and unproven; career risk

### 4. Collaborating with Newell (1954)
**Context**: Met at RAND; shared interests in decision-making
**Decision**: Commit to long-term collaboration on thinking machines
**Impact**: Founded AI; cognitive science; Nobel-winning research
**Synergy**: Neither could have achieved alone what they did together

### 5. Pursuing AI Despite Skepticism (1950s)
**Context**: Computers in infancy; AI considered science fiction
**Decision**: Continue Logic Theorist and GPS development
**Impact**: Established symbolic AI tradition; founded field
**Perseverance**: Required persistence against doubters

### 6. Staying at CMU (1949-2001)
**Context**: Offers from other institutions over decades
**Decision**: Remain at Carnegie Mellon despite opportunities
**Impact**: Built world-class programs; trained generations
**Loyalty**: Commitment to institution he helped create

### 7. Learning Chinese (1970s)
**Context**: Interest in China; Cold War opening
**Decision**: Begin Chinese language study in his 50s
**Impact**: Academic exchanges; Chinese Academy membership
**Risk**: Time away from mainstream research

## Intellectual Decisions

### Interdisciplinary Focus
**Decision**: Work across boundaries despite professional costs
**Rationale**: Problems don't respect disciplinary borders
**Impact**: Unique breadth; Nobel-Turing combination
**Challenge**: Harder to establish reputation in any single field

### Empirical Grounding
**Decision**: Insist on comparing models to human data
**Impact**: Cognitive science methodology; rigorous AI
**Trade-off**: Slower progress than purely theoretical approaches

### Satisficing Over Optimizing
**Theoretical decision**: Propose satisficing as descriptive theory
**Impact**: Behavioral economics foundation; organization theory
**Resistance**: Decades before mainstream acceptance

## Research Strategy Decisions

### Protocol Analysis Commitment
**Decision**: Use think-aloud protocols as primary data
**Impact**: New methodology for studying cognition
**Criticism**: Questioning validity of verbal reports
**Response**: Developed rigorous standards (with Ericsson)

### GPS Long-Term Investment
**Decision**: 15-year project with Newell on single system
**Impact**: Comprehensive theory; "Human Problem Solving"
**Risk**: Putting many eggs in one basket

### Scientific Discovery Programs
**Decision**: Apply AI to rediscovering scientific laws
**Impact**: Computational philosophy of science
**Scope**: Extended influence to philosophy and history of science

## Personal Philosophy Decisions

### Generosity with Ideas
**Pattern**: Sharing concepts freely rather than guarding them
**Impact**: Multiplied influence; collaborative culture
**Example**: Satisficing used by countless researchers without attribution concerns

### Student-Centered Mentorship
**Decision**: Treat students as junior colleagues
**Impact**: Extraordinary student success rate
**Examples**: Feigenbaum, Anderson, Klahr, many others

### Balanced Life
**Decision**: Maintain music, family, diverse interests alongside work
**Impact**: Longevity; sustained productivity into 80s
**Contrast**: All-work approaches leading to burnout

## Institutional Decisions

### Building GSIA/CMU
**Decision**: Invest time in institution-building
**Impact**: Premier research university; model for others
**Personal cost**: Less time for direct research

### Accepting Defense Funding
**Context**: RAND work; CMU contracts
**Decision**: Continue defense-related research
**Rationale**: Basic research with multiple applications
**Later reflection**: Some concern about military applications

## Decision-Making Style

Simon's decisions consistently showed:
- **Problem focus**: Following questions across boundaries
- **Collaborative instinct**: Seeking partnerships
- **Long-term perspective**: Multi-decade commitments
- **Intellectual courage**: Challenging orthodoxy
- **Empirical grounding**: Testing ideas against reality

## Counterfactual Analysis

**If Simon had stayed in political science**: Might have been prominent political scientist but missed AI and economics contributions

**If Simon had not met Newell**: Economics and organization theory contributions still likely; AI founding would have been different

**If Simon had moved to a major economics department**: Greater economics influence earlier; less interdisciplinary breadth

Actual decisions maximized both disciplinary depth and interdisciplinary breadth, producing unique contributions across fields.
