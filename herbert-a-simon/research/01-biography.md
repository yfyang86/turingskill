# Herbert A. Simon: Biography

## Early Life and Education
Herbert Alexander Simon was born on June 15, 1916, in Milwaukee, Wisconsin. His father was an electrical engineer who had immigrated from Germany; his mother was a pianist. Simon attended Milwaukee public schools and developed an early interest in science and mathematics. He entered the University of Chicago in 1933, earning a BA in political science in 1936. He remained at Chicago for graduate studies, receiving his PhD in political science in 1943 under the direction of Charles Merriam and Chester Barnard.

## Early Career and Administrative Behavior (1936-1949)
Simon held research positions at UC Berkeley and Illinois Institute of Technology. His 1947 book "Administrative Behavior" revolutionized organization theory by introducing concepts of bounded rationality and satisficing. The book argued that human decision-making in organizations was limited by cognitive constraints, challenging classical economic assumptions of perfect rationality.

## Move to Carnegie Tech (1949)
In 1949, Simon joined the Graduate School of Industrial Administration (GSIA) at Carnegie Institute of Technology (now Carnegie Mellon University). He would remain at Carnegie Mellon for the rest of his career, helping transform it into a major research university.

## The Logic Theorist and AI Founding (1955-1956)
Simon's collaboration with Allen Newell began in 1954. Together they developed the Logic Theorist (1955-1956), which Simon famously called "the first machine that thinks." Their work at the 1956 Dartmouth Conference effectively launched artificial intelligence as a field. Simon's 1957 prediction that computers would beat humans at chess within 10 years was bold (and slightly premature - it took 40 years).

## Nobel Prize in Economics (1978)
Simon received the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences "for his pioneering research into the decision-making process within economic organizations." He was the first Nobel laureate also to win the Turing Award (1975 with Newell). His Nobel citation highlighted bounded rationality, satisficing, and organizational decision-making.

## Contributions Across Fields
Simon's research spanned multiple disciplines:
- **Economics**: Bounded rationality, behavioral economics foundations
- **Psychology**: Cognitive science, problem-solving research
- **Computer Science**: AI, list processing, heuristic search
- **Management**: Organization theory, decision-making
- **Philosophy of Science**: Scientific discovery, heuristics

## Later Career (1980-2001)
In his later years, Simon continued active research and writing. His 1991 autobiography "Models of My Life" reflected on his interdisciplinary career. He remained engaged in cognitive science, AI, and Chinese scholarship (he studied Chinese and visited China multiple times). He became a foreign member of the Chinese Academy of Sciences in 1994.

## Honors and Recognition
- 1975: ACM Turing Award (with Allen Newell)
- 1978: Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences
- 1986: National Medal of Science
- 1993: APA Award for Outstanding Lifetime Contributions to Psychology
- 1995: Dwight Waldo Award (public administration)
- 24 honorary doctorates

## Personal Life and Death
Simon married Dorothea Pye in 1938; they had three children. He maintained diverse interests including music (piano), art, and Chinese culture. He died on February 9, 2001, in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, at age 84, following surgery complications.

## Character and Philosophy
Simon described himself as a "monomaniac" focused on human decision-making and problem-solving across different contexts. His interdisciplinary approach was methodological: use whatever tools illuminate the problem, regardless of disciplinary boundaries. He valued empirical rigor and was known for his generosity in collaborating with and mentoring younger researchers.
