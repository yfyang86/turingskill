# Alfred V. Aho: Biography

## Early Life and Education

Alfred Vaino Aho was born on August 9, 1941, in Timmins, Ontario, Canada. He grew up in a working-class mining town, developing an early interest in mathematics and science.

Aho pursued his higher education at the University of Toronto, where he earned his bachelor's degree. He then moved to the United States for graduate studies at Princeton University, earning his Master's and PhD degrees in Electrical Engineering/Computer Science.

It was at Princeton where Aho met Jeffrey Ullman in 1963—while waiting in the registration line on their first day of graduate school. This chance meeting began a collaboration that would span over five decades and revolutionize computer science.

## Bell Labs (1967-1995)

After earning their doctorates (Aho in 1967), both Aho and Ullman joined Bell Labs, AT&T's legendary research center. They worked together from 1967 to 1969, during which they laid the foundations for their future contributions to compiler theory and algorithms.

### Key Contributions at Bell Labs
- **AWK Programming Language**: Co-created with Peter Weinberger and Brian Kernighan (the "A" stands for Aho)
- **YACC (Yet Another Compiler Compiler)**: Parser generator still used today
- **egrep**: Extended grep with regular expression improvements
- **Aho-Corasick Algorithm**: String-matching algorithm (with Margaret Corasick)

Aho remained at Bell Labs for nearly 30 years, eventually becoming Vice President of Computing Sciences Research.

## Columbia University (1995-present)

In 1995, Aho transitioned to academia, joining Columbia University's Computer Science Department as the Lawrence Gussman Professor. At Columbia, he:
- Served as Chair of the Computer Science Department
- Taught courses on programming languages, compilers, and algorithms
- Mentored a new generation of computer scientists
- Continued collaboration with Ullman despite geographical distance

## The Dragon Book

Aho's most famous contribution to computer science education is *Compilers: Principles, Techniques, and Tools*, commonly known as the "Dragon Book" due to its cover illustration.

- **1977**: First edition with Ullman (*Principles of Compiler Design*)
- **1986**: Second edition with Ullman and Ravi Sethi
- **2007**: Third edition with Ullman, Sethi, and Monica Lam

The book has educated generations of computer scientists and remains the standard reference on compiler design.

## Other Textbooks

### *The Design and Analysis of Computer Algorithms* (1974)
Co-authored with John Hopcroft and Jeffrey Ullman, this book became the standard algorithms text for decades.

### *Foundations of Computer Science* (1992)
Comprehensive introduction to CS co-authored with Ullman.

## Awards and Recognition

- **IEEE John von Neumann Medal** (2003)
- **NEC C&C Foundation Prize** (parallel with Ullman)
- **Turing Award** (2020, shared with Jeffrey Ullman)
- Member of US National Academy of Engineering
- Fellow of ACM, IEEE, Bell Labs, AAAS
- Member of American Academy of Arts and Sciences
- Member of Royal Society of Canada

## Teaching Philosophy

Aho learned an important lesson from Richard Hamming at Bell Labs: "If you want to be a great scientist, you have to do more than great work; you have to teach others how to use your work."

This philosophy shaped Aho's approach throughout his career—the combination of research, writing books, and teaching.

## Personal Life

Aho is known for his modest demeanor and collaborative spirit. His long partnership with Ullman, despite working at different institutions for decades, exemplifies his commitment to scientific collaboration over institutional boundaries.
