# Charles P. Thacker: Biography

## Early Life and Education
Charles Patrick Thacker was born on February 26, 1943, in Pasadena, California. Growing up in Southern California during the post-war era, Thacker developed an early fascination with electronics and computing. He attended the California Institute of Technology (Caltech), earning his Bachelor's degree in Physics in 1967. His physics background would shape his engineering approach—combining theoretical understanding with practical implementation.

## Early Career: Berkeley and SDS (1967-1970)
After Caltech, Thacker joined the University of California, Berkeley, working on the Project Genie timesharing system. This experience introduced him to:
- Interactive computing
- Display systems
- Hardware-software co-design

He then moved to Scientific Data Systems (SDS), where he continued working on display systems and computer architecture.

## Xerox PARC: The Golden Era (1970-1983)
In 1970, Thacker joined the newly formed Xerox Palo Alto Research Center (PARC), where he would do his most influential work. At PARC, he was part of an extraordinary team that would invent the future of computing.

### The Alto (1973)
Thacker led the hardware design of the Alto, the first computer designed to support a graphical user interface:
- **Bit-mapped display**: Enabled WYSIWYG and graphics
- **Mouse input**: Pointing device integration
- **Local network connection**: Ethernet interface
- **Removable storage**: First floppy disk drive

The Alto became the prototype for personal computing. Every modern computer traces its lineage to Thacker's design.

### Ethernet (1973-1976)
Working with Robert Metcalfe and others, Thacker contributed to Ethernet:
- Coaxial cable physical layer
- CSMA/CD protocol implementation
- Network interface design
- Early networked computing applications

### The Dorado and Commercial Systems
Thacker designed follow-on systems including:
- **Dorado**: High-performance ECL-based workstation
- **Dandelion**: Commercial version of Alto concepts
- **Dolphin**: Interim commercial system

## DEC Systems Research Center (1983-1997)
In 1983, Thacker moved to Digital Equipment Corporation's Systems Research Center (SRC). At DEC, he:
- Led hardware research
- Developed high-performance workstations
- Worked on Alpha processor systems
- Continued networking research

## Microsoft Research (1997-2010s)
In 1997, Thacker joined Microsoft Research, where he worked until his retirement:

### Tablet PC (2000s)
Thacker contributed to early Tablet PC development at Microsoft, returning to the pen-based computing concepts he pioneered at PARC.

### Beehive (2000s)
Developed a multiprocessor system for research, demonstrating his continued interest in hardware innovation.

### Late Research (2010s)
Even in his 70s, Thacker remained active:
- Computer architecture research
- Mentoring hardware researchers
- Contributing to Microsoft's hardware strategy

## 2009 Turing Award
Thacker received the 2009 ACM A.M. Turing Award "for the pioneering design and realization of the first modern personal computer—the Alto—and for contributions to Ethernet and the tablet computer." The award recognized his unique combination of hardware engineering excellence and vision for how computers should work.

## Technical Style
Thacker was known for:
- **Elegant simplicity**: Clean, understandable designs
- **Practical focus**: Building systems that actually worked
- **Breadth**: Hardware, software, networking, displays
- **Collaboration**: Thriving in team environments
- **Mentorship**: Developing next-generation hardware engineers

## Honors and Recognition
- 2009: ACM Turing Award
- 2007: IEEE Emanuel R. Piore Award
- Fellow of ACM, IEEE, AAAS
- Member of National Academy of Engineering
- Member of American Academy of Arts and Sciences
- National Medal of Technology and Innovation (2007)

## Personal Characteristics
Colleagues describe Thacker as:
- Brilliant but humble
- Collaborative rather than competitive
- Hands-on engineer, not just theorist
- Generous with time and ideas
- Pragmatic problem-solver

## Death and Legacy
Thacker died on June 12, 2017, at his home in Palo Alto, California. He was 74. His legacy lives in every personal computer, every network connection, every graphical interface we use today.

## Historical Significance
Thacker represents the hardware engineering tradition that made modern computing possible. While others imagined the future, Thacker built it—turning concepts into working systems that transformed the world.
