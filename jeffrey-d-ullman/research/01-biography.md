# Jeffrey D. Ullman: Biography

## Early Life and Education

Jeffrey David Ullman was born on November 22, 1942. He grew up with a strong interest in mathematics and science, eventually pursuing computer science in its early days as an academic discipline.

Ullman earned his Bachelor's degree from Columbia University in 1963. He then entered Princeton University for graduate studies, where he met Alfred Aho while waiting in the registration line on their first day. They would become lifelong collaborators.

At Princeton, Ullman earned his Master's and PhD degrees in Computer Science (PhD completed in 1966).

## Bell Labs (1966-1969)

After completing his doctorate, Ullman joined Bell Labs as a member of the technical staff. He worked there from 1966 to 1969, overlapping with Aho for two years (1967-1969).

During this period:
- Developed foundational compiler algorithms with Aho
- Began work that would become the Dragon Book
- Established research patterns that would continue for decades

## Princeton University (1969-1979)

Ullman left Bell Labs for academia, joining the faculty at Princeton University in 1969.

At Princeton:
- Built research program in automata theory and formal languages
- Developed early database theory
- Wrote foundational textbooks
- Mentored PhD students including future Google co-founder Sergey Brin (who left before completing his degree)

However, Ullman became frustrated by Princeton's marginalization of computer science. The university prevented establishment of a separate CS department, treating computing as a service to other disciplines rather than a field worthy of independent study.

## Stanford University (1979-present)

In 1979, Ullman accepted a position at Stanford University, which had one of the strongest and best-established computer science departments.

At Stanford:
- Stanford W. Ascherman Professor of Computer Science
- Department Chair (1990-1994)
- Established the InfoLab database research group
- Continued collaboration with Aho despite geographic distance
- Mentored numerous PhD students

## Database Research

At Stanford, Ullman shifted focus to database systems, becoming one of the founders of database theory:

### Key Contributions
- **Principles of Database Systems** (1980): Revolutionary textbook
- **Lossless joins**: Formal foundations for database normalization
- **Datalog**: Logical programming for database querying
- **Data integration**: Techniques for merging data from multiple sources

### InfoLab Research Group
With colleagues including Héctor García-Molina and Jennifer Widom, Ullman built a leading database research group focused on:
- Data integration and cleaning
- Data mining techniques
- Web data management
- Parallel and distributed databases

## Gradiance Corporation

Ullman founded Gradiance Corporation, an online learning platform for computer science topics. The company developed:
- Automated homework grading systems
- Interactive learning materials
- Online courses based on his textbooks

## Writing Advice

Ullman is known for practical advice to authors:
> "Never take more than a year to write a book. If you do, the reason you are writing it will have passed."

This reflects his pragmatic approach to academic productivity.

## Personal Style

Ullman is known for:
- Rigorous mathematical approach
- Practical focus on working systems
- Dedication to teaching and textbook writing
- Long-term collaboration with Aho
- Willingness to shift research areas (automata → compilers → databases)
