# Andrew Chi-Chih Yao - Interviews and Dialogues

## Turing Award Occasion (2000)

### On Receiving the Award
"I am deeply honored to receive this recognition. Theoretical computer science is a field where elegance and practical impact can coexist, and I am grateful to have contributed to its development."

### On Yao's Principle
"The minimax principle emerged from trying to understand the power of randomness in computation. It was surprising that such a simple connection existed between randomized and deterministic complexity."

### On Communication Complexity
"Communication is fundamental to distributed computing. Understanding its inherent costs helps us design better algorithms and systems."

## Kyoto Prize Interview (2017)

### On Career Path
"My transition from physics to computer science was driven by a fascination with the fundamental nature of computation. Physics asks about the universe; computer science asks about the limits of information processing."

### On Returning to China
"After decades in the United States, I felt a calling to contribute to China's scientific development. Tsinghua offered the opportunity to build something meaningful."

### On Education
"The next generation of researchers will solve problems we cannot yet imagine. Our role is to give them the tools and the freedom to explore."

## Tsinghua University Interviews

### On IIIS Founding (2005)
"We wanted to create an institute where interdisciplinary research could flourish, where physicists, biologists, economists, and computer scientists could collaborate on fundamental problems."

### On Research Philosophy
"Good theory is timeless. While applications change, fundamental understanding remains valuable."

### On Chinese Students
"Chinese students have tremendous talent and work ethic. What they need is exposure to the frontiers of research and the confidence to tackle hard problems."

## Conference Q&A Sessions

### On Randomness in Computation
"Randomness is a powerful tool, but understanding when it is necessary and when it can be eliminated is crucial for both theory and practice."

### On Quantum Computing
"Quantum computing challenges our classical intuitions about computation. Understanding its power and limitations is one of the great problems in theoretical computer science."

### On Open Problems
"There are still fundamental questions about the relationship between randomness, communication, and computation that we do not understand."

## Academic Dialogues

### With Students
Former students recall:
- "Professor Yao always asked: 'What is the fundamental question here?'"
- "He would sit with us for hours working through proofs"
- "His standards were high, but he was patient and kind"
- "He emphasized understanding over publication"

### With Colleagues
Colleagues describe:
- "Yao listens more than he speaks"
- "When he does speak, it is worth listening"
- "He brings mathematical rigor to every discussion"
- "Collaboration with him raises everyone's standards"

## Public Lectures

### On Future of Computing
"The future of computing lies in understanding fundamental limits—whether quantum, biological, or classical. Theory provides the roadmap."

### On International Collaboration
"Science knows no borders. The best research happens when talented people from different backgrounds work together."

### On Theory vs Practice
"Theory and practice are not opposed. Good theory enables good practice, and practical challenges inspire new theory."

## Rare Personal Comments

### On Leaving Princeton
"It was a difficult decision. Princeton had been my academic home for nearly two decades. But the opportunity to build something in China, to contribute to its educational development, was compelling."

### On Identity
"I am Chinese by birth, American by training, and a scientist by profession. These identities complement rather than conflict."

### On Success
"Success in research requires patience, persistence, and a bit of luck. The most important thing is to work on problems that matter."

## Conversation Patterns

### Socratic Approach
- Asks probing questions
- Guides to insight rather than providing answers
- Encourages independent thinking
- Values the process of discovery

### Mathematical Precision in Speech
- Chooses words carefully
- Qualifies statements appropriately
- Distinguishes between what is known and conjectured
- Acknowledges limitations of current understanding

### Humility
- Downplays personal achievements
- Emphasizes collaborative nature of science
- Credits predecessors and contemporaries
- Open about what remains unknown

## Teaching Dialogues

### On Difficult Concepts
"Don't worry if you don't understand immediately. These concepts took the community years to develop. The important thing is to keep asking questions."

### On Research Problems
"Choose problems that you find beautiful, not just important. The beauty will sustain you through the difficult periods."

### On Failure
"Most attempts fail. That is the nature of research. Learn from each attempt, and eventually you will succeed."
