# Leslie Valiant: Biography

## Early Life and Education
Leslie Gabriel Valiant was born on March 28, 1949, in Budapest, Hungary. His family emigrated to England in the 1950s, where he grew up and received his education. Valiant developed an early interest in mathematics and logic, which would become the foundation of his groundbreaking work in theoretical computer science.

He attended King's College, Cambridge, earning his Bachelor's degree in Mathematics in 1970. He continued at Cambridge for graduate studies, receiving his Diploma in Computer Science in 1973 and his Ph.D. in 1974 under the supervision of Mike Paterson. His doctoral work established him as a rising star in computational complexity theory.

## Early Career and Key Contributions
Valiant held positions at:
- University of Leeds (1974-1976)
- University of Edinburgh (1975-1976)
- University of Warwick (1976-1982)

During this period, he made foundational contributions to computational complexity theory that would transform the field.

## The Turing Award Trifecta
Valiant made three contributions, any one of which could have justified a Turing Award:

### 1. PAC Learning (1984)
Valiant defined Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) learning, providing the first rigorous mathematical framework for machine learning. This established computational learning theory as a distinct field and provided foundations for modern machine learning.

### 2. #P-Completeness (1979)
Valiant proved that computing the permanent of a matrix is #P-complete, establishing the hardness of counting problems and creating the field of counting complexity.

### 3. Parallel Computing (1990s)
Valiant developed the bulk synchronous parallel (BSP) model, providing a foundation for understanding parallel computation that influenced parallel algorithm design and programming models.

## Harvard Career (1982-Present)
In 1982, Valiant joined Harvard University as the T. Jefferson Coolidge Professor of Computer Science and Applied Mathematics. At Harvard, he has:
- Built a world-class theoretical computer science group
- Continued foundational research
- Mentored generations of students
- Expanded work into neuroscience and evolution

## Later Research Directions
In recent decades, Valiant has pursued:
- **Neuroidal models**: Computational neuroscience
- **Evolvability**: Mathematical theory of evolution
- **Memorization and learning**: Cognitive computation models

## 2010 Turing Award
Valiant received the 2010 ACM A.M. Turing Award "for transformative contributions to the theory of computation, including the theory of probably approximately correct (PAC) learning, the complexity of enumeration and of algebraic computation, and the foundations of parallel and distributed computing."

Colleagues describe Valiant as:

## Legacy
Valiant's career demonstrates how fundamental theoretical research can:
- Create entirely new fields (computational learning theory)
- Transform existing fields (complexity theory)
- Enable practical technologies (machine learning)
- Bridge disciplines (computer science, neuroscience, biology)

He represents the best of theoretical computer science—rigorous, original, and broadly impactful.
