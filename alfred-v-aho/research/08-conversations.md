# Alfred V. Aho: Interviews and Dialogues

## On Meeting Jeffrey Ullman

> "We met while waiting in the registration line on our first day of graduate school at Princeton University in 1963. Neither of us knew then that this chance meeting would lead to a collaboration spanning over five decades."

## On Bell Labs

> "Bell Labs was a unique environment where researchers were given the freedom to pursue long-term projects. The management trusted that good science would eventually lead to valuable applications."

## On Richard Hamming's Advice

> "In my first week at Bell Labs, Richard Hamming told me that if you want to be a great scientist, you have to do more than great work; you have to teach others how to use your work. I found this combination of doing research, writing books, and teaching a very fruitful combination."

## On the Dragon Book

> "The Dragon Book was meant to bridge the gap between the theory of compiler construction and the practical realities of building compilers. We wanted students to understand not just why compilers work, but how to build them."

## On AWK

> "AWK was designed to make text processing simple. We wanted a tool that would allow programmers to write one-liners for common tasks. The fact that it's still used decades later shows that the need for simple text processing hasn't gone away."

## On Industry vs. Academia

> "Bell Labs taught me the value of building systems that work in practice. Columbia taught me the importance of educating the next generation. Both perspectives are essential for computer science."

## On Long-term Collaboration

> "Jeff [Ullman] and I continued working together even after we were at different institutions. We'd meet regularly, discuss ideas, and gradually our books came together. Geography doesn't have to be a barrier to collaboration."

## On Compiler Research

> "Compilers are interesting because they sit at the intersection of theory and practice. You need to understand formal languages, algorithms, and computer architecture, but ultimately you have to produce working code."

## On Teaching

> "Writing textbooks is a form of teaching that scales. A classroom lecture reaches tens of students; a textbook reaches tens of thousands."

## On Receiving the Turing Award

> "It's an honor to share this award with my longtime collaborator Jeffrey Ullman. The citation mentions our books, which reflects our belief that education is as important as research."

## On the Future of Compilers

> "Compilers continue to evolve. With new architectures like GPUs and TPUs, with machine learning models being deployed at scale, the challenges of translating high-level descriptions to efficient machine code are as relevant as ever."

## On Computer Science Education

> "Computer science is unique in how quickly the field changes. Textbooks need to balance enduring principles with current practice. That's why we've updated the Dragon Book three times over four decades."

## Advice to Young Researchers

> "Find problems that are both theoretically interesting and practically important. And don't underestimate the value of clear exposition—your work has impact only if others can understand and build upon it."

## On Unix Philosophy

> "The Unix philosophy of small, composable tools was revolutionary. AWK fit into this philosophy—it did one thing (text processing) and did it well, integrating with pipes and other tools."
