# Avi Wigderson: Interviews and Dialogues

## On Theoretical Computer Science

> "Theoretical computer science is an exciting discipline that attracts some of the most promising young researchers to work on the most difficult challenges. It has connections to all other areas of mathematics and science."

## On Randomness

> "Understanding the role of randomness in computation is fundamental. Our work showed that randomness is either extremely powerful or completely unnecessary—there's no middle ground."

## On the Nisan-Wigderson Generator

> "With Noam, we showed that if certain computational problems are truly hard, then randomness can be efficiently simulated. This connection between hardness and randomness has become a central paradigm."

## On Hardness vs. Randomness

> "The key insight is that computational hardness—problems that are difficult to solve—can be used to create pseudorandomness. This is counterintuitive but powerful."

## On Expander Graphs

> "Expanders are graphs that are sparse but highly connected. With Omer and Salil, we developed the zig-zag product, a new way to construct these graphs that led to beautiful applications."

## On Receiving the Abel Prize

> "Sharing the Abel Prize with László Lovász was an incredible honor. It recognized not just our individual work, but the deep connections between computer science and mathematics."

## On the Turing Award

> "I was surprised and delighted to receive the Turing Award. It's recognition of the field of theoretical computer science and the wonderful collaborators I've worked with over the years."

## On Collaboration

> "I've been fortunate to work with nearly 200 co-authors. Science is a collaborative endeavor, and the best ideas often emerge from discussions and joint thinking."

## On the IAS

> "The Institute for Advanced Study provides an ideal environment for deep thinking. Being here, where Einstein and von Neumann worked, is inspiring and humbling."

## On Mathematics and CS

> "Computer science is a young field, but it has already made profound contributions to mathematics. The interactions go both ways—mathematics provides tools for CS, and CS provides new questions for mathematics."

## On Open Problems

> "P vs. NP remains the central open problem. While we don't know the answer, the attempt to solve it has led to incredible advances in our understanding of computation."

## On Derandomization

> "The dream is to show that P = BPP—that every efficient randomized algorithm can be converted to an efficient deterministic one. We're not there yet, but we've made remarkable progress."

## On Teaching and Mentoring

> "Working with students and postdocs is one of the most rewarding aspects of research. Seeing them develop their own ideas and make their own discoveries is a joy."

## On the Future of TCS

> "Theoretical computer science will continue to evolve, finding new connections to physics, biology, economics, and other fields. The fundamental questions about computation are timeless."

## Advice to Young Researchers

> "Work on problems you find beautiful and important. Don't be discouraged by difficulty—the hardest problems often lead to the most interesting mathematics. And collaborate generously."
