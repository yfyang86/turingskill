# Avi Wigderson: Biography

## Early Life and Education

Avi Wigderson was born in 1956 in Haifa, Israel, to Holocaust survivors. Growing up in Israel, he developed an early interest in mathematics and problem-solving.

Wigderson completed his undergraduate studies at the Technion – Israel Institute of Technology, graduating in 1980. He then moved to the United States for graduate studies at Princeton University, where he earned his Master's degrees in Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, followed by his PhD in Computer Science in 1983.

His doctoral thesis focused on computational complexity theory, particularly the role of randomness in computation—a theme that would define his career.

## Hebrew University (1986-1999)

After completing his PhD, Wigderson joined the faculty of the Hebrew University of Jerusalem in 1986. He spent 13 years building a world-class theoretical computer science research group.

At Hebrew University:
- Established Israel as a center for theoretical computer science
- Collaborated with Noam Nisan and Michael Ben-Or
- Produced foundational work on randomness and complexity
- Mentored students who became leading researchers

## Institute for Advanced Study (1999-present)

In 1999, Wigderson joined the Institute for Advanced Study (IAS) in Princeton, New Jersey, one of the world's most prestigious research institutions (former home of Einstein, von Neumann, Gödel).

At IAS:
- Herbert H. Maass Professor in the School of Mathematics
- Leads theoretical computer science and discrete mathematics programs
- Hosts postdocs and visiting researchers from around the world
- Continues producing groundbreaking research

## Awards and Recognition

Wigderson is the only person to win both the Abel Prize (mathematics) and the Turing Award (computer science):

- **Nevanlinna Prize** (1994): For contributions to mathematical aspects of computer science
- **Gödel Prize** (2009): For outstanding papers in theoretical computer science
- **Knuth Prize** (2019): For distinguished contributions to the foundations of computer science
- **Abel Prize** (2021): Shared with László Lovász
- **Turing Award** (2023)

## Honors

- ACM Fellow

## Research Focus

Wigderson's work spans:
- **Computational complexity theory**: What can and cannot be computed efficiently
- **Randomness in computation**: The power and limitations of random algorithms
- **Cryptography**: Foundations of secure computation
- **Interactive proofs**: Verification through interaction
- **Graph theory**: Algorithms and combinatorics
- **Circuit complexity**: Lower bounds and hardness

## Personal Style

Wigderson is known for:
- Deep mathematical insight
- Willingness to work on hard problems
- Collaborative approach (nearly 200 co-authors)
- Mentorship of young researchers
- Bridging mathematics and computer science

## Philosophy

Wigderson views theoretical computer science as:
> "An exciting discipline that attracts some of the most promising young researchers to work on the most difficult challenges."

He emphasizes the deep connections between computer science and mathematics, and the fundamental nature of computational questions.
