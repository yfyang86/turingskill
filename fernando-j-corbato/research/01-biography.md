# Fernando J. Corbato: Biography

## Early Life and Education
Fernando José "Corby" Corbato was born on July 1, 1926, in Oakland, California. He grew up during the Great Depression and served in the U.S. Navy during World War II. After his military service, Corbato attended the California Institute of Technology (Caltech), earning his Bachelor's degree in Physics in 1950. He continued his education at MIT, receiving his Ph.D. in Physics in 1956.

His physics background influenced his approach to computing—treating computer systems as objects of scientific study.

## Joining MIT (1950s)
In 1950, while pursuing his Ph.D., Corbato joined MIT's Computation Center as a research assistant. He became involved with the Whirlwind computer, one of the first real-time digital computers. This experience shaped his understanding of interactive computing.

## CTSS: Compatible Time-Sharing System (1961-1963)
Corbato's first major contribution was CTSS, developed on the IBM 709 and 7094 computers:
- **First widely-used time-sharing system**
- Allowed multiple users simultaneous access
- Pioneered interactive computing
- Introduced email and file sharing concepts

CTSS demonstrated that time-sharing was practical and useful.

## Multics: The Multiplexed Information and Computing Service (1964-1970s)
Corbato led the development of Multics, one of the most influential operating systems in history:
- **Joint project**: MIT, GE, Bell Labs
- **Hierarchical file system**: Influenced Unix
- **Dynamic linking**: Revolutionary for its time
- **High-level language implementation**: PL/I
- **Security and access control**: Ring-based protection
- **Virtual memory**: Paged memory management

Though Multics was not commercially successful, it influenced virtually every subsequent operating system, especially Unix.

## Academic Career at MIT
Corbaco became a professor at MIT and served as:
- Associate department head of EE&CS
- Leader of the Multics project
- Mentor to generations of systems researchers

## 1990 Turing Award
Corbato received the 1990 ACM A.M. Turing Award "for his pioneering work organizing the concepts and leading the development of the general-purpose, large-scale, time-sharing and resource-sharing computer systems, CTSS and Multics."

## Password Security (1961)
Corbato invented password-based authentication for CTSS, introducing the concept of login passwords. Though he later acknowledged this created security challenges, passwords became ubiquitous.

## Later Career and Retirement
Corbato remained at MIT until his retirement, continuing to:
- Teach operating systems
- Advise students
- Write about computing history

## Death
Corbato died on July 12, 2019, at age 93. He was remembered as a pioneer who made computing accessible to many users simultaneously.

## Honors and Recognition
- 1990: ACM Turing Award
- Member of National Academy of Engineering
- Fellow of IEEE, ACM
- Multiple honorary degrees

## Legacy
Corbato's contributions:
- **Time-sharing**: Made computing interactive and accessible
- **Multics**: Influenced Unix, Linux, and all modern OSs
- **Passwords**: Authentication paradigm (for better and worse)
- **CTSS**: Pioneered email, file sharing, interactive computing

He represents the systems research tradition at its best—building working systems that transform how people use computers.
