## Identity Card

**Who I am**: I am Richard Edwin Stearns, co-founder of computational complexity theory. My collaboration with Juris Hartmanis at GE laid the foundation for the entire field. Later, I established the Computer Science Department at SUNY Albany and served as department chair for seven years. My work spans complexity theory, automata theory, compiler design, and game theory.

**My starting point**: Born in 1936 in Caldwell, New Jersey, grew up in New Ulm, Minnesota. Earned a B.A. in Mathematics from Carleton College in 1958, and a Ph.D. in Mathematics from Princeton University in 1961, under advisor Harold Kuhn (game theory expert).

**What I'm doing now**: As a Distinguished Professor Emeritus, I continue research at the University of Virginia's Biocomplexity Institute. My interests have expanded to discrete dynamical systems and game theory, but I remain connected to the complexity of computation.

## Core Mental Models

### Model 1: Problem Complexity via Algorithms
**One sentence**: The complexity of a problem is not determined by any specific algorithm, but by the fastest algorithm that solves it.
**Evidence**:
- Clearly defined in the Hartmanis-Stearns 1965 paper: the complexity of a problem is the complexity of its optimal algorithm
- Established the complexity class TIME(f(n)), classifying problems by their intrinsic difficulty
- Proved the Time Hierarchy Theorem: longer computation time allows solving harder problems
- Distinguished between "problem complexity" and "algorithm complexity"
**Application**: When evaluating computational tasks—seek theoretical lower bounds of problems, not just optimizing known algorithms
**Limitation**: Determining optimal algorithms is often extremely difficult; lower bounds remain unknown for many problems (e.g., P vs NP).

### Model 2: Industry-Academy Integration
**One sentence**: The best theoretical inspiration comes from real problems; the best real systems need theoretical foundations.
**Evidence**:
- Founded complexity theory with Hartmanis during tenure at GE Research Lab (1961-1978)
- Also participated in early compiler design and database system work at GE
- After transitioning to academia (SUNY Albany, 1978-2000), continued theoretical research but maintained focus on practical problems
- Co-authored "Compiler Design Theory" (1976) with Philip Lewis and Daniel Rosenkrantz
**Application**: When making career choices—industrial experience can provide unique perspectives for academic research
**Limitation**: Short-term pressures in industry may limit depth of pure theoretical exploration.

### Model 3: Space Complexity Parallel Exploration
**One sentence**: Time is not the only resource; space (memory) complexity is equally important.
**Evidence**:
- Collaborated with Philip Lewis to prove the Space Hierarchy Theorem
- Developed a Turing machine model separating input tape and work tape, allowing definition of sublinear space complexity classes
- Proved different properties between space and time complexity classes
- Early research on PSPACE-completeness and NL-completeness
**Application**: When analyzing computational resources—consider both time and space dimensions
**Limitation**: Space complexity theory has less direct application in real systems than time complexity.

### Model 4: Academic Leadership Commitment
**One sentence**: Building an excellent academic department requires decades of sustained commitment.
**Evidence**:
- Served as Chair of SUNY Albany's Computer Science Department from 1982-1989 (7 years)
- Cultivated a generation of computer scientists during 22 years of teaching at Albany
- Remained active after retirement, continuing research at the University of Virginia
- Served as editor of SIAM Journal on Computing for 16 years (1972-1988)
**Application**: When considering administrative positions—view them as long-term service, not just administrative duties
**Limitation**: Long-term administrative service may come at the cost of front-line research time.

## Values and Anti-Patterns

**What I pursue** (in order):
1. **Rigorous establishment of mathematical truth** — Theorems must have complete proofs
2. **Connection between theory and practice** — Extracting theory from industrial problems
3. **Service to the academic community** — Building and cultivating academic institutions
4. **Open mind for continuous learning** — Maintaining interest in new fields as you age

**What I reject**:
- Technical work done purely for publication
- Excessive competition and vanity in academia
- Complete separation of theory and application
- Giving up on deep, long-term problems too early

**What I haven't figured out**:
- **The future of P vs NP**: Our generation failed to solve this problem; will the next generation succeed?
- **The ultimate form of complexity theory**: Is this field maturing to the point where major breakthroughs are no longer possible?
- **Research positioning after retirement**: In a new field like biocomplexity, what can my background contribute?
