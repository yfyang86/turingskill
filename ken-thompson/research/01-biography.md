# Ken Thompson - Biography

## Early Life and Education

Kenneth Lane Thompson was born on February 4, 1943, in New Orleans, Louisiana. Growing up in the American South during the post-war era, Thompson developed an early fascination with electronics and computing machinery. His childhood interest in ham radio and electrical systems would later evolve into a passion for computer systems.

Thompson pursued higher education at the University of California, Berkeley, enrolling in the Electrical Engineering and Computer Science program. The vibrant academic environment at Berkeley in the early 1960s exposed him to cutting-edge computing research. He earned his Bachelor's degree and continued for graduate studies, completing his Master's degree in 1965 under the supervision of renowned computer scientist Butler Lampson.

His master's thesis work at Berkeley involved the development of the Berkeley Timesharing System (BTS), an experience that would prove invaluable for his later Unix work. The technical challenges of building operating systems in academic environments shaped his approach to system design.

## Bell Labs Era (1966-2000)

In 1966, Thompson joined Bell Telephone Laboratories in Murray Hill, New Jersey. The legendary research institution provided an unprecedented environment for creative exploration. At Bell Labs, he became part of the Computing Techniques Research Department, working alongside other brilliant minds including Dennis Ritchie.

The Multics project brought Thompson and his colleagues to the forefront of operating system research. When Bell Labs withdrew from Multics in 1969, Thompson found himself with access to a PDP-7 minicomputer and time to pursue his own ideas. This fortuitous circumstance led to the creation of Unix, initially developed as a personal project to run a space travel game Thompson had written.

Thompson spent over three decades at Bell Labs, becoming one of the institution's most celebrated researchers. His work during this period fundamentally shaped modern computing through Unix, the B language, regular expressions, and numerous other innovations.

## Post-Bell Labs Career

Following the restructuring of AT&T in 2000, Thompson transitioned to Entrisphere, a telecommunications equipment company. This period represented a brief but significant shift from pure research to commercial telecommunications technology development.

## Google Era (2006-2021)

In 2006, Thompson joined Google as a Distinguished Engineer. This marked a return to systems research and development on an unprecedented scale. At Google, he collaborated with Rob Pike and Robert Griesemer to design and implement the Go programming language, released publicly in 2009.

The Go language project represented Thompson's continued commitment to practical systems programming. Drawing from decades of experience with C and Unix, the team designed Go to address modern software engineering challenges including concurrency, build times, and code maintainability.

Thompson retired from Google in 2021, concluding a remarkable fifty-five-year career in computing.

## Personal Interests: Chess

Beyond computing, Thompson is an accomplished chess player and programmer. He achieved the title of International Master in chess and developed a deep interest in computer chess. His Belle chess machine, developed in the 1970s and 1980s, became the first computer system to achieve master-level play.

The Belle project combined his expertise in hardware design with his chess knowledge, featuring special-purpose hardware for move generation and evaluation. This work earned him recognition in both computer science and chess communities, demonstrating his unique ability to bridge theoretical and practical domains.

Thompson's chess endgame databases, developed using Belle, proved certain positions that had puzzled human analysts for centuries. His 1992 endgame tablebases solved all chess positions with five pieces or fewer, definitively establishing perfect play for these endgames.

## Character and Working Style

Colleagues describe Thompson as a quiet, intense, and deeply practical engineer. Unlike many researchers who pursued theoretical computer science, Thompson focused on building working systems. His code was known for its elegance, efficiency, and reliability.

The Unix philosophy of small, composable tools doing one thing well reflects Thompson's personal approach to problem-solving. He preferred simple solutions over complex ones and demonstrated that practical elegance often outperforms theoretical sophistication.
