# Ken Thompson - Conversations and Interviews

## Structured Interviews

### "Coders at Work" with Peter Seibel (2009)
This extensive interview provides the most comprehensive record of Thompson's thinking. Seibel's questions covered Thompson's entire career trajectory, from Berkeley graduate student to Google Distinguished Engineer.

**Key topics discussed:**
- Creation of Unix on the PDP-7 and the space travel game motivation
- Development of the B language and transition to C with Dennis Ritchie
- Bell Labs research culture and the freedom to pursue interesting problems
- The pipe concept and Unix philosophy emergence
- Chess programming and the Belle machine development
- Views on C++ ("it's just a garbage heap of ideas")
- Unicode and UTF-8 design over dinner with Rob Pike
- Go language development at Google and design philosophy

**Notable quotes:**
On code longevity: "We've had bits of code that have been running for 20 years... the stuff that you wrote last week—you have no idea if it's going to run for 20 years."

On complexity: Thompson expressed concern about software becoming too complex for humans to understand, a theme recurring throughout the interview.

### ACM Turing Award Interview Series
Thompson participated in retrospective interviews following the 1983 Turing Award, discussing the Unix creation process and collaboration with Dennis Ritchie. These recordings capture the early history before it became mythologized.

### CHM Oral History (Computer History Museum)
Multiple recording sessions with Thompson are archived at the Computer History Museum:
- Early computing experiences at Berkeley
- Bell Labs environment in the 1960s-70s
- Technical decision-making in Unix development
- Relationship with colleagues including McIlroy, Ritchie, and Pike

## Conference Presentations and Q&A

### Usenix Conferences
Thompson regularly presented at Usenix conferences during the Unix era. Recordings and transcripts capture:
- Early Unix evolution presentations
- Plan 9 introduction and demonstrations
- Technical debates with other operating system researchers

The question-and-answer sessions often reveal more than prepared remarks, showing Thompson's problem-solving approach in real-time.

### Google Tech Talks (2007-2010)
During his Google tenure, Thompson participated in internal and public tech talks:
- Go language announcement and rationale
- Panel discussions on programming language design
- Retrospectives on Unix and systems research

These talks often featured extended Q&A where Thompson responded to detailed technical questions about Go's design decisions.

### Bell Labs Internal Presentations
Historical records include Thompson's presentations to Bell Labs colleagues:
- Unix progress reports to management
- Technical seminars on kernel implementation
- Chess machine demonstrations

These internal communications, preserved in Bell Labs archives, show Thompson explaining work in progress to knowledgeable peers.

## Documented Conversations with Collaborators

### Dennis Ritchie Collaborations
Interviews with both Thompson and Ritchie, conducted separately and together, reveal their collaborative dynamic:
- Mutual respect and complementary skills
- Decision-making process for Unix design choices
- The B-to-C language transition
- Shared views on software simplicity

The divergence in their recollections—minor and usually about timing—provides insight into how memory shapes technical history.

### Rob Pike Partnership
Pike has spoken extensively about working with Thompson:
- Plan 9 collaboration dynamics
- UTF-8 design dinner at the diner
- Go language development at Google
- Thompson's coding habits and working style

Pike describes Thompson as intensely focused, capable of solving overnight problems that others struggled with for weeks.

### Brian Kernighan Recollections
Kernighan's writings and interviews include observations about Thompson:
- Thompson's preference for working code over presentations
- The creation of Unix documentation and man pages
- Unix philosophy development through practice rather than doctrine

## Colleague Testimonials

### Doug McIlroy on Thompson
McIlroy, who managed the Computing Techniques Research Department, described Thompson's unique productivity: "Ken would disappear for a few days and return with working code that solved problems we'd been discussing for weeks."

### Bell Labs Peers
Various Bell Labs researchers have documented their interactions with Thompson:
- His legendary overnight problem-solving
- Preference for demonstrating rather than explaining
- Deep knowledge of hardware and software boundaries
- Chess playing during compilation waits

## Topics Recurring in Conversations

**Unix Creation Stories**
Multiple interviews cover the Unix origin myth, with Thompson consistently crediting circumstances—the unused PDP-7, Bell Labs' research freedom—over personal genius.

**Software Complexity**
Across decades of interviews, Thompson returns to concerns about software becoming unmaintainable. This theme connects his early Unix work to his later Go language development.

**Bell Labs Culture**
Thompson consistently praises Bell Labs' research environment, crediting the institution's support for enabling work that might not have happened elsewhere.

**Chess and Computing**
Thompson speaks about computer chess with enthusiasm rare in other topics, describing hardware design challenges and the satisfaction of solving endgame positions.

## Gaps and Missing Records

Not all significant conversations were recorded:
- Early Unix design discussions at Bell Labs
- Informal collaborations that shaped technical decisions
- Private communications with Ritchie about C development

These gaps mean some aspects of Thompson's work must be reconstructed from code and written documentation rather than direct testimony.
