# Allen Newell: Biography

## Early Life and Education
Allen Newell was born on March 19, 1927, in San Francisco, California. His father was a professor at Stanford University, providing an academic environment for his upbringing. Newell attended Stanford, earning a bachelor's degree in physics in 1949. He pursued graduate studies in mathematics at Princeton University (1949-1950), where he encountered game theory through von Neumann and Morgenstern's work.

## The RAND Corporation Years (1950-1961)
Newell joined the RAND Corporation in Santa Monica as a research scientist. This proved transformative:
- Worked on Air Force air defense systems
- Studied logistics and organizational simulation
- Met Herbert A. Simon in 1954, beginning their legendary collaboration
- Attended Oliver Selfridge's 1954 seminar on pattern recognition, which convinced him intelligent adaptive systems were possible

## The Logic Theorist and Birth of AI (1955-1956)
With Simon and RAND colleague Cliff Shaw, Newell developed the Logic Theorist (1955-1956):
- First program designed to mimic human problem-solving
- Proved theorems from Principia Mathematica
- Introduced list processing and heuristic search
- Presented at the 1956 Dartmouth Conference, effectively launching AI as a field

## Graduate Studies and Move to CMU (1956-1961)
Inspired by his collaboration with Simon, Newell enrolled at Carnegie Institute of Technology (now CMU):
- PhD in industrial administration (1957)
- Dissertation on the chess program NSS (Newell, Shaw, Simon)
- Joined CMU faculty in 1961

## The General Problem Solver (1957-1972)
Newell and Simon developed GPS, their most influential system:
- Domain-independent problem solver
- Means-ends analysis
- Cognitive simulation of human reasoning
- Published in "Human Problem Solving" (1972)

## Cognitive Architecture Research (1960s-1970s)
Between major projects, Newell pursued diverse research:
- Speech recognition systems
- Computer architecture (CMU's C.mmp multiprocessor)
- Human-computer interaction (work with Stu Card and Tom Moran)
- Management information systems

## The SOAR Project (1980s-1992)
Newell's culminating achievement:
- Began 1983 with John Laird and Paul Rosenbloom
- Unified theory of cognition based on production systems
- Integrated problem-solving, learning, and perception
- Chunking mechanism for automatic learning
- Designed to outlast him as a research vehicle

## Institutional Building
Newell was instrumental in:
- Transforming CMU's Psychology Department into a premier cognitive science center
- Creating CMU's School of Computer Science (SCS)
- Establishing one of the first campus-wide computer networks (1982)
- Building the CMU AI research community

## Honors and Awards
- 1971: Harry Goode Award (AFIPS)
- 1975: ACM Turing Award (with Herbert Simon)
- 1985: Distinguished Scientific Contribution Award (APA)
- 1987: William James Lectures at Harvard
- 1992: National Medal of Science
- Member: National Academy of Sciences, National Academy of Engineering

## Personal Life and Death
Newell married Noël; they had a son, Paul. He died on July 19, 1992, in Pittsburgh, at age 65, from cancer. His final book, "Unified Theories of Cognition" (1990), synthesized his life's work toward a comprehensive cognitive architecture.

## Character and Philosophy
Newell described himself simply as "a scientist." His work embodied:
- Commitment to understanding human cognition through computational modeling
- Belief in unified theories over isolated models
- Rigorous empirical methodology
- "Choose a final project that will outlast you" - his advice and his practice with SOAR
