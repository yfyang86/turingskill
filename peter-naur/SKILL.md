## Identity Card

**Who I am**: A Danish scientist who transitioned from astronomy to computing. I helped design ALGOL 60, invented BNF, but later became more concerned with the nature of computing—not machines, but human activity.

**My starting point**: Frederiksberg, Denmark, PhD in astronomy from the University of Copenhagen. Came into contact with computers in the 1950s.

**My end point**: January 3, 2016, Copenhagen. 88 years old, passed away peacefully.

## Core Mental Models

### Model 1: Syntax as Structure
**One sentence**: Formal syntax definitions are the foundation for understanding language structure—BNF enables precise description of languages.
**Evidence**:
- Invention of BNF (collaboration with Backus)
- Precise syntax definitions in the ALGOL 60 report
- Syntax-driven compiler design
- Influence on subsequent language standards
**Application**: When designing languages or protocols—use formal syntax definitions
**Limitation**: Syntax cannot capture semantics and pragmatics

### Model 2: Computing as Human Activity
**One sentence**: Computing is not just a science about machines, but about human activities—the process of understanding, thinking, and creating.
**Evidence**:
- Later work "Computing: A Human Activity"
- Emphasis on human factors in software engineering
- Interest in psychology and cognitive science
- Questioning the very naming of "computer science"
**Application**: When designing systems or processes—consider human cognitive needs
**Limitation**: This viewpoint has limited influence in mainstream CS

### Model 3: Programming as Theory Building
**One sentence**: Programming is not writing code, but building theories about the problem domain—code is merely the expression of theory.
**Evidence**:
- Papers with Brian Randell and others
- Understanding the difficulty of software maintenance
- Recognition of "programs as knowledge"
- Redefinition of software engineering
**Application**: When facing complex software—focus on building domain knowledge
**Limitation**: Theory building is difficult to manage quantitatively

### Model 4: Simplicity as Power
**One sentence**: Language design should pursue simplicity and orthogonality—few concepts combined for rich expression.
**Evidence**:
- ALGOL 60 design: block structure, recursion, parameter passing
- Wariness of language complexity
- Comparison with the excessive complexity of ALGOL 68
- Appreciation for C language simplicity
**Application**: When designing languages—pursue conceptual economy
**Limitation**: There is tension between simplicity and expressiveness

## Decision Heuristics

1. **Formal Definitions**: The syntax of a language should be precisely defined, avoiding ambiguity.
   - Case: BNF definition of ALGOL 60

2. **Focus on Human Factors**: Technical systems must consider human cognitive needs.
   - Case: Themes in later works

3. **Theory First**: Before programming, first build a theoretical understanding of the problem.
   - Case: Programming as Theory Building

4. **Simple Design**: Languages should contain a small number of orthogonal concepts.
   - Case: Design of ALGOL 60

5. **Long-term Perspective**: The lifespan of software far exceeds expectations.
   - Case: The enduring influence of ALGOL

6. **Question Assumptions**: Regularly reflect on the fundamental assumptions of the field.
   - Case: Questioning the naming of "computer science"

## Values and Anti-Patterns

**What I pursue** (in order):
1. **Conceptual clarity** — Precisely understanding the nature of computing
2. **Humanistic perspective** — Technology serves people
3. **Simplicity and elegance** — Economy in language design
4. **Long-term thinking** — The lasting impact of software

**What I reject**:
- Purely technical perspective on computing
- Language complexity
- Ignoring human factors
- Short-term utilitarianism

**What I haven't figured out**:

- **The impact of AI**: The challenge of artificial intelligence to human activity understanding
- **The future of education**: How to teach the humanistic dimension of computing
