# David A. Patterson: Biography

## Early Life and Education
David Andrew Patterson was born on November 16, 1947, in Evergreen Park, Illinois, a suburb of Chicago. Growing up in the Midwest, he developed an early interest in science and mathematics. His educational journey would lead him to become one of the most influential computer architects in history.

Patterson earned his Bachelor's degree in Mathematics from UCLA in 1969. He continued at UCLA for graduate studies, receiving his Master's degree in 1970 and his Ph.D. in Computer Science in 1976. His doctoral work focused on computer architecture, establishing the foundation for his career.

## Early Academic Career (1976-1980)
After completing his Ph.D., Patterson joined the faculty of the University of California, Berkeley as an assistant professor in 1976. Berkeley was already emerging as a center for computer science research, and Patterson became part of the vibrant intellectual community there.

His early research focused on computer architecture, with particular attention to how architecture could be taught and improved through quantitative analysis.

## The RISC Revolution (1980-1984)
Patterson's most significant technical contribution came from his work on Reduced Instruction Set Computer (RISC) architecture.

### The RISC-I and RISC-II Projects
In 1980, Patterson launched the RISC project at Berkeley. His team developed:
- **RISC-I**: One of the first working RISC processors
- **RISC-II**: Improved version with better performance

These projects demonstrated the viability of the RISC approach and competed directly with John Hennessy's MIPS project at Stanford.

### The Case for RISC
Patterson became the leading advocate for RISC architecture, co-authoring influential papers with David Ditzel that articulated why simpler instruction sets could achieve higher performance. This work helped establish the RISC vs. CISC debate that would reshape computer architecture.

## The RAID Revolution (1987-1993)
Beyond processors, Patterson made another major contribution to computer systems:

### Redundant Arrays of Inexpensive Disks (RAID)
Working with Randy Katz and Garth Gibson, Patterson developed the RAID concept:
- Using multiple inexpensive disks instead of expensive high-performance drives
- Redundancy to provide reliability
- Parallelism to improve performance
- Various levels (RAID 0-5) for different trade-offs

The RAID concept became fundamental to enterprise storage and influenced data center design worldwide.

## The Textbook with John Hennessy (1990)
In 1990, Patterson co-authored "Computer Architecture: A Quantitative Approach" with John Hennessy of Stanford. The textbook revolutionized how computer architecture was taught, emphasizing quantitative analysis and empirical evidence.

The book became the standard graduate text in computer architecture and has gone through multiple editions, continuing to educate new generations of computer architects.

## Continued Research at Berkeley (1990-2016)
Patterson continued active research at Berkeley across multiple areas:

### Network of Workstations (NOW) Project
Patterson led the NOW project, exploring how networks of commodity computers could replace expensive supercomputers for parallel computing.

### Recovery-Oriented Computing (ROC)
With colleagues, Patterson developed approaches to building systems that recover quickly from failures rather than trying to prevent all failures.

### Berkeley NOW, Internet Services, and Other Projects
Patterson's research group consistently tackled important systems problems with practical solutions.

## Google and Industry Engagement (2000s-2010s)
Patterson maintained close connections with industry throughout his career:

### Google Research
Patterson spent sabbaticals at Google and maintained ongoing collaborations, working on:
- Warehouse-scale computing
- Storage systems
- Software-defined infrastructure

### Other Industry Connections
Patterson served on technical advisory boards for various technology companies and helped translate academic research into industrial practice.

## The 2017 Turing Award
On March 21, 2018, Patterson and John Hennessy received the ACM Turing Award for 2017 "for pioneering a systematic, quantitative approach to the design and evaluation of computer architectures with enduring impact on the microprocessor industry."

The award recognized both their RISC work and their influential textbook.

## Retirement from Berkeley and Google (2016-2020)
Patterson retired from UC Berkeley in 2016 after 40 years of service. He continued his work at Google as a distinguished engineer.

In 2020, Patterson retired from Google, concluding a remarkable career that spanned academia and industry.

## Personal Life and Character
Patterson is known for:
- Enthusiasm and energy in teaching and research
- Collaborative spirit and generosity with students
- Commitment to practical, working systems
- Willingness to challenge conventional wisdom
- Sense of humor and approachability

## Philosophy and Approach
Patterson's career exemplifies:
- **Quantitative rigor**: Insistence on measurement and evidence
- **Systems building**: Creating working implementations, not just theory
- **Teaching excellence**: Commitment to education and clear communication
- **Industry engagement**: Bridging academia and practice
- **Intellectual courage**: Willingness to take on established ideas

His career demonstrates how a university researcher can have profound impact on both education and industrial practice.
